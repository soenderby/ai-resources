# Agent Planning Patterns

Two questions, related but distinct:

1. **Agents as planners**: LLMs generate plans, but their first drafts are often bad. Why, and what patterns improve the output?
2. **Plans for agents**: Given a plan (human-authored or otherwise), what makes it something an agent can execute well?

The existing collection covers the second question reasonably well — Harper Reed's three-loop workflow, NK's "plan before coding," Klaassen's three-fidelity model, Willison's TDD-as-planning. The first question was thinner. This document fills both gaps with new research.

## Part 1: Why LLM-generated plans are often bad

### The structural problem

Subbarao Kambhampati's research group at Arizona State has produced the most rigorous evidence on LLM planning limitations. Their position, backed by multiple papers and benchmarks, is stark: **LLMs cannot plan, but can help planning.**

The argument has three parts:

1. **LLMs don't reason about subgoal interactions.** Planning requires understanding that actions have preconditions and effects, and that these interact — doing step A may make step B impossible, or step C may need to happen before step D even though D was conceived first. LLMs generate plans that *look* reasonable (the right steps in roughly the right order) but fail at execution because they don't track these interactions. In coding terms: the agent produces a plan where step 3 depends on a refactoring that step 5 will do, or where two steps silently modify the same state.

2. **LLMs cannot reliably verify their own plans.** This is the deeper problem. Even when given a candidate plan and asked "is this correct?", LLMs perform poorly — they have high false-positive rates and miss most true negatives. This means **self-critique alone is unreliable for plan improvement.** The model is as likely to "correct" a good plan into a bad one as to fix an actual flaw.

3. **Many claims of LLM planning ability rest on hidden external verification.** When researchers report that Tree-of-Thought or Reflexion "improves" planning, Kambhampati's group shows the improvement typically comes from an external verifier (a simulator, a test suite, arithmetic checks) doing the actual verification work, while the LLM contributes candidate generation. The LLM's contribution is generating plausible guesses, not doing sound reasoning.

A recent counterpoint: Bohnet et al. (Dec 2025) showed that with carefully constructed few-shot and many-shot examples plus iterative self-critique, LLMs can achieve significant planning improvements on Blocksworld benchmarks *without* external verifiers. This suggests the picture may be more nuanced than Kambhampati's strong claim — but the results are on formal planning domains, and it's unclear how they translate to the messier domain of software planning.

### What this means for coding agents

The implication is not that agents can't help with planning — they clearly can, and do — but that **the value they provide is idea generation, not sound reasoning.** An agent asked to plan a feature implementation will produce a plan that captures many of the right ideas (because it has seen thousands of similar plans in training data) but may miss critical interactions, dependencies, or constraints.

Common failure modes in agent-generated coding plans:

- **Over-optimistic scope**: the plan doesn't account for the actual complexity of integration
- **Missing dependency ordering**: steps are listed in an order that doesn't account for what each step needs from previous steps
- **Conflating separate concerns**: the plan bundles unrelated changes into a single step, making each step too large and the diff unreviewable
- **Ignoring unstated constraints**: the plan violates architectural conventions, team norms, or performance requirements that weren't explicitly stated in the prompt
- **Shallow decomposition**: the plan lists high-level goals ("implement authentication") rather than concrete, verifiable steps ("create user registration endpoint that validates email format and returns 201 with user ID")

### What actually works for improving agent-generated plans

Given that self-critique alone is unreliable, the patterns that work all introduce **external signal** into the refinement loop:

**1. External verification (the LLM-Modulo pattern)**

Kambhampati's proposed architecture: the LLM generates candidate plans, and a bank of external critics evaluate them. Critics can be:
- **Hard critics**: tests, type checkers, linters, build systems — anything that gives a definitive pass/fail
- **Soft critics**: a different LLM instance evaluating style, architecture, or adherence to conventions (less reliable but still useful)
- **Human critics**: the developer reviewing the plan before execution

The critiques are pooled and fed back to the LLM as a "backprompt" to generate the next candidate. The loop continues until all hard critics sign off. The soundness guarantee comes entirely from the critics, not from the LLM.

In practice for coding agents, this maps to: **generate a plan → run it past tests/linters/type checkers (hard critics) → run it past a human or second model (soft critics) → revise → repeat.** The key insight is that the LLM is the idea generator, not the verifier.

**2. Domain-specific "cognitive architectures" (flow engineering)**

Harrison Chase (LangChain) observes that general-purpose planning improvements (plan-and-solve, Reflexion, CoT) are "often too general for practical use by agents in production." What actually works is building a **domain-specific flow** — a hardcoded sequence of steps that tells the agent what to do and in what order, removing the planning burden from the LLM.

The best example is AlphaCodium (Ridnik et al., 2024), which achieves a dramatic improvement in code generation (GPT-4 accuracy from 19% to 44% on competitive programming) by replacing a single prompt with a multi-stage flow:
1. Reflect on the problem
2. Generate public tests
3. Generate an AI-designed solution
4. Iterate on the solution against the tests
5. Generate additional tests
6. Continue iterating

The key principles: structured decomposition into stages, test anchors for verification, soft decisions with double validation, encouraging exploration and postponing direct decisions, modular code generation.

Chase's framing of why this works is clarifying: building a domain-specific flow is essentially **doing the planning for the LLM.** "We are basically saying: 'don't worry about planning, LLM, I'll do it for you!' Of course, we're not removing ALL planning from the LLM, as we still ask it to do some planning in some instances." The engineer's knowledge of what a good planning sequence looks like gets encoded in the flow, not left to the model.

**3. Human review of the plan artifact**

The simplest and most widely practiced pattern. NK Schjødt's framing: in the old world, quality assurance happened at code review. In the agentic world, **the plan is the primary artifact of human judgment.** Review the plan, not the code.

Osmani's workflow makes this explicit: "I often iterate on this plan — editing and asking the AI to critique or refine it — until it's coherent and complete. Only then do I proceed to coding." This plan-critique-refine loop uses the human as the external verifier, which is slower but higher-quality than automated critics for architectural and design decisions.

NK's **pre-mortem** technique is a specific variant: ask a fresh session to assume the plan has catastrophically failed and diagnose why. This uses a different model instance with no sunk-cost bias, which often catches specification gaps the original session missed.

**4. Disposable prototypes as planning tools**

Klaassen's three-fidelity model identifies a category of work — genuinely uncertain problems — where planning is premature because the problem itself isn't well enough understood. The pattern: let the agent build a disposable prototype to explore the problem space, learn from the result, discard the code, and then write the real plan from a position of understanding rather than speculation.

This is underappreciated. It inverts the standard advice of "plan before you code" for cases where the planning input is insufficient. The prototype's purpose is not to produce code but to produce *knowledge that enables a good plan.*

## Part 2: Creating plans that agents can execute well

### The ExecPlans format (the current gold standard)

Aaron Friel at OpenAI developed the ExecPlans format — a detailed specification for planning documents that enabled Codex to work autonomously for **over seven hours** from a single prompt. The format was refined through extensive use and has been adopted by practitioners reporting significantly longer successful agent sessions (from minutes to 30+ minutes and beyond).

The core design principle: **a plan should be self-contained enough that a stateless, amnesiac agent can pick it up cold and execute it.** The agent has no memory of prior plans and no external context — only the working tree and the ExecPlan file.

Key properties of the ExecPlans format:

**Self-containment is non-negotiable.** Every ExecPlan must contain all knowledge and instructions needed for a novice to succeed. No references to external blogs or docs; if knowledge is required, embed it in the plan. Define every term of art in plain language. Repeat any assumption you rely on. This is critical because agents lose context across sessions, and even within sessions, earlier context may be lost to attention degradation.

**Purpose and intent come first.** Begin by explaining why the work matters from a user's perspective: what someone can do after this change that they could not do before, and how to see it working. Then guide through the exact steps.

**Observable outcomes, not code descriptions.** Acceptance should be phrased as behavior a human can verify ("after starting the server, navigating to /health returns HTTP 200 with body OK") rather than internal attributes ("added a HealthCheck struct"). If a change is internal, explain how its impact can still be demonstrated.

**Living documents.** ExecPlans must be updated as progress is made, discoveries occur, and decisions are finalized. Required sections include Progress (checkboxes with timestamps), Surprises & Discoveries (unexpected findings with evidence), Decision Log (every decision with rationale), and Outcomes & Retrospective.

**Prototyping milestones are encouraged.** When a plan involves significant unknowns, explicit prototyping milestones de-risk the work. Keep prototypes additive and testable. Clearly label scope as "prototyping" and state criteria for promoting or discarding.

**Prose over checklists.** "Write in plain prose. Prefer sentences over lists. Avoid checklists, tables, and long enumerations unless brevity would obscure meaning." This is counterintuitive — you'd think agents would prefer structured formats — but the reasoning is that prose forces the plan author to make connections and reasoning explicit rather than hiding them in terse bullet points.

### The spec-driven development pattern

GitHub's Spec Kit formalizes a four-phase workflow:

1. **Specify**: describe what you're building and why (user journeys, success criteria — not technical details)
2. **Plan**: provide technical direction (stack, architecture, constraints); agent generates detailed plan
3. **Tasks**: agent breaks spec + plan into small, independently testable work units
4. **Implement**: agent tackles tasks one by one; developer reviews focused changes

The key design insight: **each phase has a specific job, and you don't move to the next one until the current task is fully validated.** This gating prevents the "house of cards" failure mode where the agent builds on an unvalidated foundation.

The Specify phase deliberately separates intent from implementation. The question is not "what framework should we use?" but "who will use this? what problem does it solve? how will they interact with it?" This separation ensures the agent doesn't make premature technical decisions that foreclose better options.

### What makes a plan step agent-executable?

Across all the sources, a few consistent properties emerge for individual plan steps that agents can execute well:

**1. Concrete and bounded.** "Create a user registration endpoint that validates email format and returns 201 with user ID" — not "implement authentication." Each step should be completable in a single agent session without ambiguity about scope.

**2. Verifiable.** Every step needs a way to confirm it's done. Tests are best ("run `npm test` and expect the new test `test_registration` to pass"). Build output is second best. Visual confirmation is weakest but acceptable ("navigate to /register and confirm the form renders").

**3. Ordered by dependency.** Earlier steps must not depend on later steps. This sounds obvious but is a frequent failure mode of agent-generated plans.

**4. Context-complete.** Each step should name the files to modify (with full paths), the functions to change, and how the change connects to what was done in previous steps. A stateless agent reading step 5 should be able to execute it knowing only what steps 1–4 produced.

**5. Right-sized.** Harper Reed's planning prompt asks the model to iterate on step granularity until each step is "small enough to be safe and big enough to be meaningful." Too small and you waste context on trivial steps. Too large and the diff is unreviewable and the agent drifts.

### The Planner-Worker separation

Cursor's scaling experiments (running hundreds of concurrent agents for weeks) discovered that flat agent coordination fails — agents become risk-averse, duplicate work, and avoid hard problems. The solution: a **Planner-Worker hierarchy.**

- **Planners** explore the codebase, create tasks, and can spawn sub-planners for specific areas (making planning recursive and parallel)
- **Workers** execute assigned tasks without worrying about the big picture
- A **Judge** assesses whether the overall goal is met at the end of each cycle

This maps onto the spec-driven development pattern at a different scale. The planner is doing the Specify/Plan/Tasks phases; the workers are doing the Implement phase. The judge is the gating check.

A key finding: **model choice matters for planning vs. execution.** Cursor found that GPT-5.2 was a better planner than GPT-5.1-Codex, even though the latter was trained specifically for coding. The skills for planning (understanding the big picture, maintaining focus, precise instruction-following) differ from the skills for execution (code generation, test writing, debugging).

## Connections to existing collection

The planning cluster connects to several existing themes:

**Design as the new bottleneck.** Maggie Appleton's observation that "Gas Town churns through implementation plans so quickly that you have to do a LOT of design and planning to keep the engine fed" is the practical consequence of the patterns described here. When execution is cheap, the quality of the plan becomes the binding constraint.

**Judgment memory.** The ExecPlans format, with its Decision Log and Surprises & Discoveries sections, is a form of within-session judgment memory. The plan accumulates decisions and their rationale, which serves both the agent (context for future steps) and the human (audit trail for review).

**The supervision cluster.** The pre-mortem technique, the plan-as-review-artifact pattern, and the gated spec-driven workflow are all responses to the same problem identified in the supervision fatigue / work intensification / comprehension debt cluster: when generation is cheap, oversight becomes the hard part. Planning is the leverage point where human oversight has the highest return per unit of effort.

**Zero Framework Cognition.** Yegge's ZFC principle suggests that planning and reasoning should be delegated to the model, not hardcoded. But AlphaCodium and the domain-specific cognitive architecture approach push back: for planning specifically, hardcoding the *structure* of the planning process (while leaving the *content* to the model) produces dramatically better results. The resolution may be that ZFC applies within each planning stage but not to the stage sequencing itself.

**Context engineering.** The ExecPlans emphasis on self-containment is an extreme form of context engineering — ensuring the plan itself is the only context the agent needs. This connects to Anthropic's context engineering principles and the broader judgment memory theme, but with a specific application: the plan document as a self-sufficient context package.
