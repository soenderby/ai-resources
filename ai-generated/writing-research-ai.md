# Writing, Research, and the Question of AI Assistance

---

## Part 1: What We Know

### Writing is thinking

Every serious source on writing converges on the same claim: writing is not the transcription of finished thought. It is the medium in which thought occurs.

Paul Graham puts it most directly: "Writing doesn't just communicate ideas; it generates them. If you're bad at writing and don't like to do it, you'll miss out on most of the ideas writing would have generated." Leslie Lamport, the computer scientist, is blunter: "If you're thinking without writing, you only think you're thinking."

This is not a metaphor. Booth, Colomb, and Williams, in *The Craft of Research*, describe the mechanism: "We write to remember more accurately, understand better, and evaluate what we think more objectively." Writing forces you to separate your ideas from the swift flow of thought and fix them where you — and others — can examine them. Ideas that felt compelling in your head turn out, on the page, to be vague or circular or missing a step. You discover this only by writing. There is no substitute.

The US Army's writing guide for field-grade officers, which draws on Zinsser and cognitive science, opens with the same principle: "Writing is thinking on paper." The guide then does something that most writing advice doesn't — it shows what this means in practice, with side-by-side comparisons of the same idea expressed vaguely and expressed clearly. The difference is not cosmetic. The clear version thinks harder.

George Orwell, in "Politics and the English Language," made the strongest version of this argument in 1946. Bad language, he wrote, enables bad thinking — and the process is reversible. "If one gets rid of these habits one can think more clearly." Orwell's concern was political: vague language lets people hide atrocities behind euphemism. But the principle applies anywhere language is used to avoid precision. Writing that reaches for the easy phrase instead of the exact thought is not just stylistically weak. It is a failure of thinking.

The implication is uncomfortable and direct: writing that skips the hard cognitive work — through vagueness, through formula, or through delegation to a tool that produces fluent prose on demand — skips the thinking.

### What makes writing useful

Knowing that writing is thinking doesn't tell you what good writing looks like. Graham offers the most testable formula: useful writing tells people something **true**, **important**, **novel to the reader**, and **stated as strongly as possible** without becoming false.

These four components multiply. An essay that is true, important, and novel but stated weakly — hedged into meaninglessness with qualifications — scores near zero. An essay that is strong and novel but wrong is worse than useless. The formula creates a productive tension: you want to make the strongest claim you can, but every increase in strength risks crossing into falsehood. "Precision and correctness are like opposing forces," Graham writes. "Vaporous academic writing is correct but vague; the bold rhetoric of demagogues is precise but false. Useful writing is bold, but true."

The Army's writing guide arrives at a similar framework through different language. It defines four standards: **substance** (the intellectual content — is the thesis coherent and supported?), **organization** (are ideas arranged logically?), **style** (is the prose clear, concise, and active?), and **correctness** (grammar, citation, formatting). These are interdependent. The guide is explicit: "Even if the substance is strong, commanders will not accept disorganized, confusing, error-ridden writing." Polished emptiness fails. Brilliant ideas in unreadable prose fail. You need all four.

### The craft of clarity

Orwell's six rules remain the most memorable constraints on style: avoid dead metaphors, prefer short words, cut mercilessly, use active voice, avoid jargon, and — the rule that saves all the others from rigidity — break any rule rather than say something barbarous. These are negative constraints. They tell you what to avoid.

Zinsser provides the positive principle. Good nonfiction writing is a personal transaction between two humans. The writer's humanity is what keeps the reader reading. Simplicity, clarity, brevity, warmth. "The essence of writing is rewriting." Most first drafts, Zinsser argues, can be cut by fifty percent without losing information or voice. The cutting is not just trimming. It is a form of thinking — choosing what matters and letting go of what doesn't.

Steven Pinker, in *The Sense of Style*, explains why writing goes wrong even when writers know the rules: the **curse of knowledge**. Once you understand something, you can no longer imagine what it's like not to understand it. You skip steps. You use abstractions that are meaningful to you but opaque to your reader. You write for the person you are, not for the person reading your words for the first time. Pinker's remedy is to adopt "classic style" — the stance of showing something to the reader, as if pointing to an object in the world, rather than lecturing from a position of authority.

The Army guide is the most concrete of all these sources. It doesn't just explain principles — it demonstrates them. Side-by-side rewrites show academic prose transformed into professional prose, with the same meaning in fewer, clearer words. The MEAL paragraph model (Main idea, Evidence, Analysis, Link back) gives writers a checkable structure for every paragraph. Signal word tables map the relationships between ideas to specific connecting words. This is the craft equivalent of Booth's research methodology: not abstract advice but workable tools.

The guide also makes a point that the other sources imply but rarely state: "This is a guide, not a rulebook. Writing is not about following rules; it is about making choices." The chapter on active voice explains several situations where passive voice is more effective. The chapter on editing advises against hyperbole — then uses a hyperbolic phrase ("murder your darlings") to make its most important point memorable. Writing well means knowing the tools and choosing the right one for each sentence.

### The craft of research

Booth, Colomb, and Williams wrote the standard guide. Their central contribution is the three-step "So What?" test:

1. I am studying X,
2. because I want to find out Y,
3. in order to help my reader understand Z.

If you can complete step 1 but not step 2, you have a topic but not a question. If you can complete step 2 but not step 3, you have a question that interests you but may not interest anyone else. Step 3 is the hardest and the most important: it forces you to articulate why your work matters beyond your own curiosity. Every annotation in a curated collection faces this test. "This article is about agent security" fails. "This article coins a model that reduces a messy security discussion to something non-specialists can use" passes.

The deeper claim in *The Craft of Research* is that research is a conversation between equals. You don't just report findings — you anticipate your readers' objections, acknowledge counterevidence, explain your warrants (the unstated assumptions that connect your evidence to your claims), and build an argument thick enough to earn trust. The ethics chapter extends this into a claim about community: concealing objections, caricaturing opposing views, and plagiarizing don't just violate rules. They damage the shared enterprise of people trying to understand something together.

Umberto Eco, in *How to Write a Thesis*, adds two practical principles that complement Booth. First: read primary sources, not summaries of summaries. Citing an article based on what another article said about it is irresponsible — the intermediate author may have misunderstood, simplified, or cherry-picked. Second: "You are not Proust. Do not write long sentences." Break them up. Name your subjects. Make every sentence carry one idea clearly.

### The craft of reading

Mortimer Adler and Charles Van Doren, in *How to Read a Book*, argue that most people read at only two of the four available levels. **Elementary** reading is basic comprehension — understanding the words on the page. **Inspectional** reading is systematic skimming — getting the structure, the main claims, and the general direction quickly. Most readers stop here.

**Analytical** reading is thorough engagement with a single work: understanding not just what the author said but what they meant, evaluating their arguments, identifying their assumptions, and forming your own assessment. **Syntopical** reading is the highest level: reading multiple works on the same subject, comparing their arguments, and constructing an understanding that no single source contains.

Syntopical reading is exactly what annotation and cross-linking in a curated collection requires. You read a new article analytically — what is the specific insight, what is the argument, what is the evidence? — and then place it syntopically against everything else in the collection. This source contradicts that one. This framework extends that idea. These three articles are about the same underlying tension, viewed from different angles. The annotations that capture these connections are syntopical acts.

The relevant question for AI-assisted curation: at what level is the human reading? If the AI drafts the annotation and the human approves it, the human may be reading inspectionally — getting the gist — while the annotation presents the appearance of analytical reading. The output looks right. But the thinking didn't happen at the level the output implies.

## Part 2: What Changes with AI

### The offloading spectrum

Jazaieri and colleagues, writing in *Frontiers in Psychology* in 2025, propose a taxonomy for what happens when humans delegate cognitive tasks to AI. They call it **cognitive offloading** and identify three levels.

**Assistive offloading** is when technology helps cognition without replacing it. A reminder app cues your memory. An outlining tool helps you organize your thoughts. A spell checker catches errors. The user retains monitoring and control. Internal processing continues. The tool is a scaffold — it supports the structure while you build it.

**Substitutive offloading** is when technology replaces cognition. The tool doesn't help you think — it thinks for you. Auto-suggest reduces the cognitive processing you apply to word choice. A generated draft replaces the struggle of turning ideas into sentences. The user reviews output instead of producing it. The critical finding: substitutive offloading creates an **illusion of competence**. Users overestimate their understanding of material they didn't generate. They believe they know what the AI produced, in the way they would know something they wrote themselves. They don't.

**Disruptive offloading** is when substitutive use becomes habitual and unexamined. Self-monitoring diminishes. Reflection loops collapse. The human optimizes for speed over comprehension without noticing the shift. At this level, the technology doesn't just replace specific cognitive acts — it reshapes the user's expectations about what cognitive effort should feel like. Difficulty becomes a signal to delegate rather than a signal to think harder.

The taxonomy is not about the tool. It's about the mode of use. The same AI can be used assistively in one moment (brainstorming a structure) and substitutively in the next (generating a draft). The same session can include both. The critical variable is whether the human retains internal monitoring and control — whether they're using the AI to extend their thinking or to replace it.

### What happens to the output

In 2024, researchers at Durham University conducted a blinded evaluation of three hundred short physics essays — half written by students, half generated by GPT-4. Five independent markers, unaware of authorship, scored them on content accuracy, logical structure, and adherence to academic standards. The result: no statistically significant quality difference. When markers subsequently tried to identify which essays were human-written, they performed only slightly better than chance.

This finding is not isolated. Across multiple studies and disciplines, AI-generated text matches or exceeds human output on structural dimensions — organization, grammar, adherence to conventions — while lagging on analytical depth, novelty of insight, and nuanced judgment. The gap is narrowest for well-structured, convention-following genres (abstracts, introductions, summaries) and widest for work that requires genuine intellectual contribution (original analysis, creative argument, deep domain reasoning).

The implication is important: if you evaluate the product alone, AI-assisted and human-written output may be indistinguishable. A reader encountering a well-annotated knowledge collection cannot tell, from the annotations, whether a human thought carefully about each source or an AI produced fluent summaries that the human approved. The quality difference — if it exists — is not in what appeared on the page. It's in what the process of writing built inside the human.

### What happens to the human

The evidence on what AI assistance does to the human — as opposed to what it does to the output — is more troubling.

The foundational finding is older than AI. In 2011, Sparrow, Liu, and Wegner demonstrated what they called the "Google Effect": when people expect information to be readily accessible online, they are less likely to commit it to memory. They remember where to find information rather than the information itself. The mechanism extends naturally to AI-assisted writing: if you know the agent will draft the annotation, you process the source material less deeply. Why struggle to understand the argument when the AI will summarize it for you?

The METR study, already in this collection, adds a sharper edge: in a randomized trial with experienced open-source developers, AI use made them 19% slower on average — despite the developers expecting a 24% speedup and still believing afterward that AI had helped. Self-assessment of AI benefit is unreliable. People believe they are being helped when the evidence says otherwise.

At Corvinus University in Budapest, a 2025 experiment assigned students randomly into AI-permitted and AI-restricted groups. Students with AI access showed disengagement and lower understanding of the material. When the AI-restricted condition was imposed, students escalated their complaints to the media and eventually to the State Secretary for Higher Education. The researchers noted that this extreme reaction demonstrated how rapidly dependency forms — a pattern consistent with the disruptive end of Jazaieri's offloading spectrum.

The most direct evidence for the writing-as-thinking problem comes from the cognitive offloading research itself. Users who rely on AI-generated answers overestimate their knowledge in Judgment of Learning tests. They show lower metacognitive accuracy over time — they become worse at knowing what they know and what they don't know. And they shift toward ease over depth, choosing to delegate tasks that feel effortful rather than engaging with the difficulty.

This maps precisely onto Osmani's concept of comprehension debt, which this collection already tracks: the growing gap between code that exists and code anyone understands. The same gap can open between annotations that exist and annotations anyone has genuinely processed. The annotations look like understanding. They may not be.

### What happens to language and thought

A 2025 study titled "AI's Social Forcefield" provides empirical evidence for a subtler effect. Across two studies, the researchers found that exposure to AI-generated language "shapes not only how people speak, but also how they think, what they attend to, and how they relate to each other." The AI's linguistic patterns don't just appear in the output — they colonize the human's subsequent thinking and communication, even when the AI is no longer present.

This is direct evidence for what the essay on this collection's evolution called "annotation drift." If a human works extensively with an AI that writes in a particular analytical voice — structured, balanced, confident — the human's own thinking and expression begin to converge on that voice. The collection develops a uniform sheen. The diversity of perspective that would come from genuinely different human readings of different sources gets smoothed into a consistent synthetic style.

Whether this is a problem depends on what the collection is for. If it's a reference tool, consistency of voice is a feature. If it's an expression of one person's evolving understanding — a garden, in Caufield's sense — then the loss of the gardener's distinctive voice is a loss of the thing that makes the garden worth visiting.

### The central tension

These findings create a tension that has no clean resolution.

On one side: AI-assisted output is often indistinguishable from human output. It is faster to produce. It is structurally clean. For a reader who wants to learn from a well-annotated collection, the origin of the annotations may not matter.

On the other side: the process of writing builds something in the writer that the process of reviewing does not. The struggle to turn vague understanding into precise prose — Zinsser's "essence of writing is rewriting" — is where ideas are tested, refined, and genuinely understood. Substituting AI output for that struggle may preserve the product while hollowing out the producer.

For a personal knowledge base, where the curator's growing understanding is the point — not just the annotations on the page — this tension is the whole question. Is the collection a product to be consumed by others, or a practice that develops the curator's judgment? If both, which takes priority when they conflict?

The honest answer is that we don't know the terms of this trade-off. The evidence is clear on the mechanisms: substitutive offloading reduces learning, creates illusions of competence, and reshapes cognition. But the evidence doesn't tell us where the line is — how much AI assistance is assistive and at what point it becomes substitutive. The same person using the same tool may cross that line without noticing.

## Part 3: What We Don't Know

The previous sections rest on established principles and empirical evidence. This section is different. It names gaps — questions that would need to be answered for the topic to be well-understood, and that current evidence does not resolve.

### No validated methodology for AI-assisted writing

Patterns are emerging. Survey data suggests that people who use AI primarily for **planning** — brainstorming, outlining, structuring — report higher ownership of the final product and greater learning engagement than those who use AI for wholesale generation. Active steering (revising, redirecting, injecting new content) correlates with more productive collaboration than passive acceptance of AI output.

These observations are plausible and consistent with the cognitive offloading taxonomy: planning-mode use looks assistive, while generation-mode use looks substitutive. But no controlled study has tested whether one approach to AI-assisted writing actually produces better outcomes than another — better in terms of the writer's learning, the quality of the output, or both. The patterns are promising observations, not validated protocols.

The CGSC guide's six-stage writing process — plan, research, draft, revise, edit, submit — was designed for human writers. Whether the same stages apply when AI participates is an open question. Perhaps AI is most valuable in the planning and editing stages (assistive) and most dangerous in the drafting stage (substitutive). Perhaps the revision stage is where AI assistance is most needed and least harmful, since the writer has already done the thinking and now needs help seeing the draft as a reader would. These are hypotheses. Nobody has tested them.

### No validated methodology for AI-assisted research

Tools for AI-assisted research exist and are multiplying: Consensus synthesizes scientific literature, Elicit automates systematic review workflows, Undermind mimics human iterative search. Karpathy's autoresearch demonstrates autonomous investigation loops where an agent runs hundreds of experiments without human intervention.

But none of these describe a tested, repeatable protocol for **collaborative** human-AI research — the kind where a human directs the investigation, an AI handles the breadth, the human evaluates what comes back, and the synthesis emerges from the interaction. How should the work be divided? Which judgments should stay human? When should you trust an AI-retrieved source, and when should you verify it independently? How do you avoid the substitutive trap — letting the AI's search define what you find, rather than your question?

The deep research paradigm, as described in a 2025 survey, identifies four stages: planning, question developing, web exploration, and report generation. But this describes what autonomous agents do, not what a human-AI research team should do. The collaborative version of this pipeline — where human judgment enters at each stage — has not been formalized or tested.

### No evidence on whether human curation adds value

The claim that human curatorial judgment produces something an AI alone cannot is intuitive and widely believed. A human curator decides what matters and what doesn't, sees connections that aren't obvious, excludes the noise, and shapes a perspective. These are acts of taste — and taste, the argument goes, can't be specified in a prompt.

This claim is plausible but unproven. Nobody has tested: given the same raw sources, does a human-curated collection produce better outcomes for a downstream user than an AI-curated one? If so, on which dimensions — accuracy, insight, navigability, taste? The Durham physics essay study suggests that for short-form writing, AI output quality is comparable to human output quality. Whether that finding extends to the more judgment-heavy task of curation — selecting, framing, connecting, excluding — is unknown.

It may be that the value of human curation is real but diminishing as AI capabilities improve. It may be that it's real and durable because it depends on something AI lacks — genuine stakes, a career, a need to understand the field rather than just index it. It may be that the value was always smaller than curators believe, and the illusion of competence documented in the offloading research applies to curators' assessment of their own contribution. All three are possible. None is established.

### No longitudinal evidence on cognitive effects

The cognitive offloading research is clear on short-term mechanisms: substitutive use reduces learning, creates illusions of competence, and degrades metacognitive accuracy. But these findings come from short-term studies — a semester, a series of experiments, a controlled writing task.

We don't know what happens over longer time horizons. Does a curator's judgment atrophy after a year of AI-assisted annotation? Or does it sharpen, because the AI handles mechanical work and frees attention for higher-level thinking? Both are plausible. The first is consistent with the substitutive offloading evidence. The second is consistent with the assistive offloading evidence. Which pattern dominates in practice depends on how the person uses the tool — and the evidence suggests that people are bad at knowing which mode they're in.

The field is too new to have produced the longitudinal studies that would answer this question. It is not too new to name it.

## What holds and what doesn't

The principles of good writing — clarity, precision, cutting, rewriting, thinking on the page — are as valid now as when Orwell articulated them in 1946. They don't depend on the tool. They depend on the cognitive engagement of the writer. A sentence written by hand, typed on a keyboard, dictated to a microphone, or drafted by an AI and revised by a human can be clear or vague, precise or woolly, useful or empty. The tool didn't decide. The writer did — or didn't.

The principles of good research — primary sources over secondary, the "So What?" test, anticipating objections, reading analytically rather than inspectionally — are similarly durable. They describe what it takes to understand something well enough to contribute to a conversation about it. AI tools can accelerate parts of the research process — scanning sources, extracting data, identifying patterns across large bodies of literature. But the judgment of what matters, what connects, and what the reader needs to understand remains a human cognitive act, and the evidence suggests that delegating it has costs.

What doesn't hold is any confident claim about how to use AI assistance well. The mechanisms of risk are well-documented: substitutive offloading reduces learning, creates illusions of competence, and reshapes cognition. The mechanisms of benefit are intuitive but empirically thin: planning-mode use looks promising, the division of breadth (AI) and depth (human) seems natural, and active steering appears to preserve more of the human's cognitive engagement than passive acceptance. These are reasonable working hypotheses. They are not established practice.

The most honest thing to say is that we are in a period where the tools are ahead of our understanding of how to use them. The traditional principles tell us what good writing and research require: genuine cognitive engagement, the willingness to struggle with difficulty, the discipline to cut and rewrite, the honesty to acknowledge what you don't know. The empirical evidence tells us that AI assistance can undermine these requirements without the user noticing. What the evidence does not yet tell us is how to capture the benefits of AI assistance — its speed, its breadth, its tirelessness — without paying the cognitive costs.

That question will eventually be answered, by controlled studies and by the accumulated experience of people who use these tools seriously and observe what happens. Until then, the traditional principles are the best guide we have — not because they were designed for this situation, but because they describe what thinking requires regardless of the situation. The tools change. The requirements of clear thought do not.
