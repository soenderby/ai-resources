# Growing the Garden

*How a curated knowledge collection could evolve — and the tradeoffs embedded in the choices already made.*

---

In 2015, Mike Caufield delivered a keynote at the Digital Learning Research Network called "The Garden and the Stream." It laid out a distinction that has shaped how a growing number of people think about knowledge on the web.

The **stream** is the dominant form: Twitter feeds, blog rolls, news alerts, email threads — information flowing past in reverse chronological order, each item responding to the last, the whole thing collapsing into a single timeline where recency is relevance. Streams are conversational, self-assertive, and immediate. They're how most of the web works.

The **garden** is the counterweight. A garden arranges information spatially, by relationship rather than by date. It's integrative rather than assertive: things are placed not because they just happened but because they connect to what's already there. Gardens grow slowly. They're tended, not published. Each visit can reveal different paths through the same material.

Caufield traced the garden model back to Vannevar Bush's 1945 essay "As We May Think," which envisioned a device called the Memex — a desk-sized machine where an individual could store and link documents through "associative trails." The core verbs of the Memex were *link, annotate, change, copy, summarize*. These are gardening verbs. Bush imagined a tool for thinking, not a tool for broadcasting.

This repository — a personal knowledge base about AI, LLMs, and agents, maintained in markdown files — is a garden in Caufield's sense. Entries are arranged by conceptual relationship, not publication date. They accumulate through annotation and cross-linking. The recent structural split into `articles.md` (durable concepts and frameworks) and `events.md` (time-bound snapshots) is a direct application of Caufield's principle: separating the topological layer from the chronological stream.

The garden metaphor also captures what the collection is not. It's not a link dump, not a newsletter archive, not a reading list. It's a deliberately tended space where every placement decision is a curatorial judgment — what belongs, what doesn't, and how things connect.

But the metaphor has limits. Real gardens are visited by people who didn't plant them. This one is primarily visited by its own gardener and by AI agents acting on the gardener's behalf. That changes what "tending" means in ways the traditional knowledge management literature doesn't address.

## The Central Tension: Sources vs. Concepts

Andy Matuschak, an independent researcher working on tools for thought, has articulated the strongest principle for knowledge organization: **notes should be concept-oriented, not source-oriented.** When you organize by concept, knowledge accumulates. New material strengthens, complicates, or contradicts what's already there. Understanding compounds. When you organize by source — one note per book, one entry per article — you get a scattered collection of summaries that don't build on each other. The concept of "comprehension debt" might appear in three different article annotations, but if those annotations live under three different source headings, the connection between them is invisible unless you happen to remember all three.

This collection is organized by source. Articles, books, people, projects — each in its own file, each entry keyed to a specific URL. The annotations extract concepts: bold terms like **supervision fatigue**, **lethal trifecta**, **context rot** serve as markers. Cross-references between entries ("a strong companion to The Human-in-the-Loop is Tired: same phenomenon, different lens") create some of the conceptual tissue. But the structure doesn't reflect concepts. To find everything the collection knows about agent security, you have to scan every annotation in every file.

This is a real structural weakness, not just an acceptable tradeoff with costs on both sides. Source-oriented organization supports browsing — *who* wrote *what*, and what's been added recently. Concept-oriented organization supports retrieval — *what do I know about X?* The collection chose browsability. The cost is that conceptual retrieval — the thing you actually need when you're trying to *think* with the collection rather than just browse it — requires reading everything or relying on memory.

Both layers could exist: source files for browsing, concept notes for retrieval. But maintaining two layers means synchronizing two layers. A new article gets added but the relevant concept note doesn't get updated. A concept note links to three sources when there are now seven. The Zettelkasten tradition sidesteps this by making concept notes the primary layer and treating source notes as transient inputs to be processed and discarded. This collection can't do that without losing the source-level browsability that makes it useful as a shared reference. That's a genuine bind, not a problem with a clean solution.

The collection already has seeds of a concept layer. The librarian-notes track clusters: "a useful cluster is forming around the idea that AI shifts the bottleneck upward rather than removing it." The ai-generated directory contains synthesis essays that draw on multiple sources. The annotations themselves use bold terms as conceptual handles. The honest question is whether these seeds are growing toward something useful or whether they're creating maintenance surfaces that nobody will tend.

## The Human-AI Curation Partnership

This garden is unusual in a way that the knowledge management literature doesn't cover. It's co-tended by a human and AI agents. Luhmann's Zettelkasten was designed for one human mind processing index cards over decades. Digital gardens in the Appleton/Caufield tradition are personal — one person's evolving understanding, published as a work in progress. This collection is maintained through a collaboration where the AI reads the sources, drafts the annotations, proposes the cross-links, and manages the file structure, while the human directs the curation, approves the output, and makes the judgment calls about what belongs.

This arrangement has failure modes that are specific to it and largely unexamined.

**Annotation drift.** The AI writes most of the annotations. Over time, they develop a consistent analytical voice — structured, balanced, confident in its assessments. But whose voice is it? If you read thirty annotations in sequence, they sound like the same author. That uniformity is a signal: either the human curator has an unusually consistent style, or the AI's voice has displaced the human's. In practice, it's almost certainly the latter. Whether this matters depends on whether the annotations reflect genuine understanding or are well-structured summaries that the human approved without deeply processing. From the outside, these two states are indistinguishable — which is part of what makes this failure mode insidious.

**False comprehension.** The AI produces a well-structured summary of an article. The human reads it, recognizes that it captures the key points, and approves it. The annotation now exists in the collection looking like understanding. But the human may not have read the original article at all, or may have skimmed it while the AI did the close reading. This is the collector's fallacy in a more sophisticated disguise: the collection grows, but the curator's actual understanding doesn't grow with it. Addy Osmani's concept of comprehension debt — originally about the gap between code that exists and code anyone understands — maps exactly to the gap between annotations that exist and annotations anyone has genuinely processed.

**Comfort-zone reinforcement.** The AI learns what the curator likes. It starts surfacing material that fits the existing perspective and quietly deprioritizing material that doesn't. The anti-hype filter, which is supposed to be a deliberate curatorial choice, becomes an echo chamber filter driven by pattern-matching rather than judgment. The collection's biases deepen without the curator noticing, because the feedback loop is invisible.

**Maintenance as performance.** Sessions where entries get added, cross-links get tidied, notes get updated, and everything looks productive — but no actual thinking happens. The AI makes this seductive because the output looks like productive work. Files change, commits accumulate, the collection grows. The Pydantic article on supervision fatigue describes exactly this dynamic in coding: the satisfying parts of the work are being automated, while the exhausting parts (genuine review, real judgment) are growing. The same applies to curation. Adding entries is satisfying. Thinking hard about whether they should be there is not.

The uncomfortable implication: the gardener faces the same challenges as the developer working with coding agents. The collection documents these challenges — comprehension debt, supervision fatigue, work intensification — for others. It hasn't reckoned with them for itself.

## The Case Against Curation

A collection that doesn't seriously engage with the arguments against its own existence isn't being honest about its purpose. Four objections deserve attention. Some can be refuted; some land.

### "This is collecting, not learning"

Christian Tietze, writing at Zettelkasten.de, named the **collector's fallacy**: gathering information feels productive but isn't the same as understanding it. You can have a beautifully annotated collection of seventy articles about AI agents and still not be able to explain the core ideas to someone over coffee without opening the file. The test, in Tietze's formulation, is whether you can produce something *new* from what you've collected. If the collection only reproduces what's in it — if the annotations are summaries rather than synthesis — then it's a filing system, not a thinking tool.

This collection has a partial defense. The ai-generated directory contains essays that draw on multiple clusters to produce something that doesn't exist in any single source. The librarian-notes track connections that emerge from juxtaposition — observations like "multi-agent for breadth-first exploration, single-agent for depth-first coherence" that arise from placing two opposing sources next to each other and noticing the resolution. This is not just collecting.

But the defense only holds if synthesis keeps happening. Accumulation is the default state; synthesis is the exception that requires deliberate effort. Any session that adds five entries and produces zero new connections is a session of pure accumulation. The risk is not hypothetical. It's the gravitational pull of the project — adding is easy, connecting is hard, and the AI makes adding even easier.

### "Just use search"

If an AI agent can search the web and synthesize on demand, why maintain a curated collection at all? This is the strongest objection. Models with million-token context windows can ingest dozens of articles on a topic and produce a synthesis in seconds. Why pre-process any of this?

Three things a curated collection does that real-time search doesn't. First, the annotations embed curatorial judgment that takes time to form: this article is worth reading *because* of this specific insight, and it connects to *that* piece in *this* way. A search finds pages; it doesn't tell you why they matter to each other. Second, the value of the collection is as much in what it *excludes* as in what it contains. The decisions that someone doesn't belong, that a popular article is actually shallow, that a speculative piece is below the bar — these negative judgments are invisible but constitute most of the curatorial work. A search engine can't replicate exclusion decisions. Third, a maintained collection carries temporal depth — the record of how understanding evolved. The librarian-notes record decisions and observations that inform future decisions. This accumulated perspective is destroyed when you start fresh each time.

But these refutations rest on an untested assumption: that the annotations are genuinely better than what an AI could produce on the fly from the raw sources. If you gave an agent the seventy original articles and asked it to produce annotations, cross-links, and a synthesis essay, would the result be noticeably worse than what's in the collection? It might not be. And if it isn't, the case for pre-curation collapses from a quality argument to an efficiency argument: it's cheaper to maintain a collection than to regenerate one, but the output is roughly equivalent.

The honest position: this project is valuable now, in the gap between what retrieval can do today and what it will be able to do eventually. The gap is real but it's closing. The collection is probably a transitional form — somewhere between Bush's Memex and whatever AI-native knowledge management will look like. That's fine. Gardens that accept their impermanence tend to be better maintained than those that assume they'll last forever.

### "Your filter is your bias"

The anti-hype filter is presented throughout this collection as a strength. Every filter is also a blindspot. This section names the blindspots not as problems to fix but as accepted costs of the chosen approach.

The collection is overwhelmingly male, US/European, practitioner-oriented, and skeptical of commercial enthusiasm. The librarian-notes flag some of this — the gender imbalance, the absence of non-Western perspectives. But flagging a bias isn't the same as correcting it, and correcting it may not be the right move. A personal knowledge base reflects a person's actual sources and interests. Forcing diversity for its own sake would make the collection dishonest. The cost is that the collection's map of the field has large blank regions.

What the filter systematically excludes:

Many of the most interesting practitioners building with AI don't write blog posts. They work in private Slacks, internal company documents, and conversations that never become URLs. A collection built from public URLs has a survivorship bias toward people who publish. This is structural — a URL-based collection *cannot* capture non-published knowledge. It's a limitation of the medium, not a correctable oversight.

Some commercial projects and announcements contain genuine signal about where the field is heading. The anti-hype filter risks dismissing the medium (marketing) along with the message (real technical direction). Willison's analysis of the OpenAI/Astral acquisition was admitted because his analysis separated signal from marketing. Many commercial developments with genuine signal won't have an analyst to interpret them, and will be filtered out.

The collection is better at tracking problems with AI — fatigue, comprehension debt, security risks, labor market erosion — than at tracking genuine breakthroughs and capabilities. Healthy skepticism can shade into reflexive dismissal. A collection that documents every cost of cheap generation but captures none of the real capabilities is as distorted as one that tracks only the hype.

These are not problems to solve. They are the shape of this collection's perspective. A different curator with different interests would produce a different collection with different blindspots. The value is in having an explicit, acknowledged perspective — not in pretending to cover everything.

### "AI will make this obsolete"

Not in the hype sense, but seriously. As model capabilities grow, as context windows expand, as retrieval-augmented generation improves — does a small human-maintained collection still serve a purpose?

Today, with current tools, yes. The collection gives a starting agent or a new reader an opinionated map of a vast and noisy space. It saves them from rediscovering what the curator already learned. But in five years? If agents can maintain their own evolving knowledge of the field — reading new publications, updating their understanding, tracking people and projects — then a human-maintained collection becomes a bottleneck rather than an enabler.

The thing that's hardest to automate is taste. The judgment that this source matters and that one doesn't, that this framing is useful and that one is misleading — taste is accumulated through experience and can't be specified in a prompt. If the collection is valuable, it's because it encodes taste. If taste can eventually be specified or learned by machines, the collection is a transitional artifact.

The honest position: this is probably a transitional form. Its value is real and present but not guaranteed to be permanent.

## What Happens as Gardens Grow

The knowledge management literature, across library science, information theory, intelligence analysis, and personal knowledge management, identifies several problems that emerge as collections scale. Not all of them apply yet, but understanding them helps distinguish between problems worth solving now and problems worth monitoring.

### Navigation breaks down

Information foraging theory, developed by Peter Pirolli and Stuart Card at Xerox PARC, borrows from optimal foraging theory in biology. Just as animals follow scent trails to find food, information seekers follow cues — titles, descriptions, link text — to estimate whether a path will lead to useful information. The strength of these cues is the "information scent." As a collection grows, the scent per entry weakens because there's more to scan and each entry's signal is diluted by the surrounding noise.

The collection's index currently lists every title. At seventy entries, this is navigable. At two hundred, it won't be. The annotations carry strong scent — bold terms, specific claims, explicit connections — but only if you read them. An agent skimming the index sees only titles.

The Obsidian personal knowledge management community has developed a pattern called **Maps of Content**: navigational hubs that orient a reader to a topic area with curated links and commentary about how entries relate. These are more than flat indexes — they add the context that makes navigation efficient. The collection doesn't have these yet, and will eventually need them.

### Staleness accumulates silently

In 1960, R.E. Burton and R.W. Kebler formalized the concept of the "half-life of knowledge" — the time during which half of the currently active literature in a field was published. Unlike radioactive decay, knowledge doesn't disintegrate; it becomes obsolescent. And different fields decay at different rates. Computer science literature has a notoriously short half-life. A field that moves as fast as AI agents has an even shorter one.

The events.md split is a structural defense: time-bound entries are separated from durable ones. But articles.md entries can also go stale. Links die. Projects get abandoned. Tools referenced in annotations get superseded. An annotation that says "the first convincing LLM agent" may not age well.

In security classification, classifiers are required to specify expected duration at the time of classification — a built-in expiration date. The collection's date convention for events is a version of this. But articles carry no dates, and the implicit assumption is "durable." That assumption won't always hold, and there's currently no mechanism to detect when it fails.

Aris Vasilopoulos, in the Codified Context paper already in this collection, documents the cost of stale knowledge in AI-assisted systems: "agents trust documentation absolutely." If an agent reads a stale entry in this collection and treats it as current, the collection has become actively misleading — worse than having no entry at all.

### The concept layer lags behind the source layer

As sources accumulate, the conceptual connections between them multiply faster than the source count grows. With seventy entries, there might be two hundred interesting cross-connections. Most of these exist only in the curator's head or in scattered librarian-notes entries that nobody systematically consults.

The Zettelkasten tradition's answer is to make concepts first-class objects: each concept gets its own note linking to the relevant sources. Over time, the concept notes become the primary navigation layer, and the source entries become evidence. Legal databases solved the same problem: the West Key Number System extracts principles from individual cases and organizes them by topic. You find the relevant case *through* its conceptual classification, not by browsing a chronological list of decisions.

But adding a concept layer introduces the synchronization problem: two things to maintain instead of one. The mitigation is to let concept notes be born from repeated need — when you find yourself searching for "everything about X" for the third time, that's the signal — rather than from a completeness impulse. The risk is that "wait for need" means "never start," because the friction of searching is absorbed silently and never triggers the creation threshold.

### The collection's boundaries blur

Every field of knowledge has adjacent fields. This collection started with AI, LLMs, and agents, expanded into software engineering quality — Parnas, Beck, Brooks, CUPID — and has now reached into knowledge management with Caufield and Matuschak. Where does it stop?

A useful lens comes from a skills durability framework published by Chief Learning Officer Magazine: **perishable** knowledge (half-life under 2.5 years) is tied to specific tools, platforms, and versions. **Semi-durable** knowledge (2.5–7.5 years) is frameworks and methodology. **Durable** knowledge (over 7.5 years) is foundational principles. The adjacent sources in this collection are almost all in the durable or semi-durable category. Parnas wrote about modular decomposition in 1972. Brooks wrote about essential complexity in 1975. These earn their place by being more lasting than the AI-specific material they contextualize.

But there's a risk: scope creep by quality. If every strong foundational paper in every adjacent field is in scope, the collection becomes a general-purpose intellectual library. "Does this illuminate AI agent work specifically?" should remain the gate — not "is this a good piece of writing?"

An honest example from this session: the Caufield and Matuschak entries are about knowledge management, not AI agents. They were added because they inform the collection's own structure. That's legitimate for a personal knowledge base — you're allowed to include things that help you think about your tools. But it's scope creep, and describing it as "adjacent to agent context engineering" is a rationalization. The essay should name this rather than pretend the boundary held.

### The garden can also die

Knowledge gardens die in four characteristic ways.

**Neglect.** Links rot, annotations reference outdated tools, the index drifts from the actual contents. The collection stays online but becomes misleading. This is the most common death for personal knowledge projects — not a dramatic collapse but a slow accumulation of small inaccuracies that eventually make the whole thing untrustworthy.

**Overproduction.** Entries accumulate faster than they can be tended. The gardener adds but never prunes, never revisits, never synthesizes. The collection becomes a filing cabinet — organized but inert. This is the collector's fallacy at structural scale: a large, well-labeled collection that nobody actually uses to think.

**Abstraction.** The meta-layers grow faster than the primary sources. Concept notes, cluster guides, generated essays, indexes of indexes. The collection becomes a commentary about a collection that nobody reads directly anymore. The map replaces the territory.

**Boredom.** The gardener's interests shift. The field stops being novel. The daily practice of reading and annotating loses its pull. This is the most honest failure mode and the hardest to plan against, because gardens depend on the sustained interest of a specific person.

The warning signs for all four are the same: sessions where nothing meaningful is added. Annotations that feel mechanical. Generated documents that nobody reads. A feed checker that runs but whose output is never acted on. The gardener skimming rather than reading.

### Where this collection is right now

The essay should examine the actual collection, not just describe risks in the abstract.

What's working: the annotations are genuinely high quality. They extract specific ideas, not just topics. The cross-linking between entries creates real information density — reading one entry leads naturally to three others. The anti-hype filter produces a collection that feels opinionated rather than exhaustive, which is the right quality for a personal knowledge base. The feed checker and agent-assisted workflow make maintenance practical.

What's showing strain: the source/concept gap is real and already felt. Finding "everything about X" requires scanning the full file or relying on the agent's memory of previous sessions. The librarian-notes "Connections" section is becoming a second, less structured index that partially duplicates information from annotations. The ai-generated directory contains documents of widely varying quality — some are thorough syntheses, others are research notes from a single session that haven't been revisited.

Where the failure modes are already present: annotation drift is arguably happening. The annotations have a consistent analytical voice that is as much the AI's as the curator's. Whether this matters depends on whether the curator is genuinely engaging with the sources or approving summaries — and from the outside, these two states look identical. Overproduction risk is moderate: seventy-six entries across articles and events after roughly two weeks of active development is fast enough that the ratio of adding to reflecting may be skewed toward adding.

## Concrete Evolution Paths

Four structural changes that might help, ordered by when they'd become valuable.

**Staleness review.** The most immediately useful addition. Extend the existing feed checker infrastructure to audit existing entries: dead links, abandoned projects with no recent GitHub activity, annotations that reference tools or model versions that no longer exist. The detection is automatable; the response requires judgment — remove the entry, update the annotation, move it to events.md? Each case is different.

**Cluster guides.** When navigation starts to strain, short documents — one per major conceptual cluster — that link to the relevant entries with brief commentary about how they relate. Agent security. Quality with agents. Orchestration patterns. Labor market effects. Judgment memory. More like annotated reading lists than essays. The design question: hand-maintained or agent-generated? Probably hand-curated with agent assistance, since the curation judgment is the value. But honestly: if the curator won't maintain six guides, zero guides is better than six stale ones.

**Topic tags.** When the collection outgrows single-file scanning, a small controlled vocabulary — fifteen terms or so — added as a line per entry. The lightest version of what library science calls faceted classification. The argument for doing it now: cheap to add per entry, expensive to backfill later. The argument for waiting: a taxonomy built from seventy entries may not suit two hundred entries, and premature categories create false precision. The honest cost of waiting: if the approach is wrong, you won't know until backfilling is painful. This is a real tradeoff, not a free lunch.

**Concept notes.** The long-term organic move. Individual notes that accumulate over time — `comprehension-debt.md`, `supervision-fatigue.md`, `lethal-trifecta.md` — each linking to the relevant sources and tracking how understanding of the concept has evolved. This is the full Matuschak/Zettelkasten move: making concepts first-class objects. The existing ai-generated documents are already proto-concept-notes. The design principle: concept notes should be born from need, not from a taxonomy exercise. When you find yourself repeatedly searching for "everything about X," that's the signal to create one.

## What to Resist

### Premature structure

Andy Matuschak: "Premature scaling can stunt system iteration." Every structural addition — tags, concept notes, guides, new directories — adds a maintenance commitment. The commitment is invisible at creation and real over time. The collection's current philosophy — "suggest structure organically, only propose new directories when there is actual content that warrants them" — is exactly right. The events.md split was warranted by a clear problem and a clean migration. The same test should apply to all future structural changes: is this solving a problem I've actually encountered, or a problem I imagine I might encounter?

### Growth itself

There is probably an optimal size for this collection, and it's not "as large as possible." A smaller, well-tended collection where every entry has been genuinely processed and connected is more valuable than a larger one where many entries are placeholders for "I should read this properly someday." The question to ask before adding an entry is not just "is this good enough?" but "will I actually think about this, or am I just filing it?" The entry may pass the quality bar but fail the purpose test.

At some point, pruning may become more valuable than planting. The sign: when new entries don't change how you think about what's already there.

### Over-formalization

Epistemic status tags, formal citation typing, maturity indicators, confidence scores — all interesting in theory, all adding notation overhead that competes with the annotation prose that makes this collection distinctive. The annotations already do informally what formal systems do with metadata: noting confidence ("more speculative than the collection's usual bar"), tracking provenance ("builds on X, contradicts Y"), signaling durability ("coins the term," "one of the foundational papers"). Prose signals are richer than any tag.

But the current approach has a cost, and the cost should be named. Prose-based signals work when a human reads every annotation. They degrade when an agent skims for relevance. An agent can filter by tag; it can't reliably filter by a phrase buried in an annotation's third sentence. If agents reading this collection are silently missing relevant entries because they can't filter by topic or confidence level, the curator may never learn about it. The collection currently optimizes for human readability at the expense of machine retrievability. As AI agents become more central to how the collection is used, this cost grows.

### Automation beyond assistance

The AI knowledge base management literature is full of automated curation and maintenance. Some of it is genuinely useful — the feed checker is a good example of the agent detecting candidates while the human decides what to act on. But most automation in this space replaces the judgment that is the collection's value.

The standard argument: the gardener's time is finite, so let the agent handle the mechanical work (dead link detection, draft annotations, format consistency) while the human focuses on the high-judgment work (what belongs, how it connects, what to cut). This is the same argument developers make for coding agents, and it's compelling.

The counterargument — that manual annotation is how the gardener develops taste, and that automating it away causes judgment to atrophy — is plausible but unproven. It might be that taste comes from reading and thinking rather than from the act of writing the annotation, and that delegating annotation would free time for the deeper work that actually builds understanding. This essay takes the position that manual curation preserves taste, but acknowledges this is a hypothesis. If it's wrong, the collection is paying a significant time cost for no benefit.

## The Garden and the Gardener

The collection doesn't need to justify its existence through grand claims. It's a personal knowledge base about a field the curator cares about, maintained with the help of AI tools, shared publicly as an experiment. Its value is practical: when the curator or an agent starts a new task, the collection provides a pre-filtered, pre-connected starting point that's better than a blank search bar.

But understanding what the gardener contributes — and what the gardener costs — matters for how the collection evolves.

What the gardener does that the agent can't:

Decide that a popular, well-written article is actually shallow and doesn't belong. Notice that two apparently unrelated entries are about the same underlying tension and write the connection. Feel that a cluster is getting overrepresented and consciously seek out counterweights. Recognize that their own perspective has shifted and revisit old annotations with fresh eyes. Stop adding for a while and just think about what's already there.

These are acts of taste. An agent can process. Taste requires having something at stake — a career, a practice, a genuine need to understand the field rather than just index it. The gardener cares whether the collection is *true*, not just whether it's *complete*.

What the gardener brings that's bad:

Inconsistency. Some annotations are thorough three-sentence syntheses that extract the key insight and connect it to three other entries. Others are perfunctory. Quality varies by session, by fatigue, by how interesting the source was. Availability bias. Recently discovered sources get disproportionate attention. Older entries that might deserve revisiting get ignored because they're not in working memory. Fatigue. Quality drops over a long session. The seventh annotation in a batch is rarely as sharp as the first. Forgetting. The curator may not remember what's already in the collection, leading to redundant additions or missed connections that a machine would catch instantly.

These are not failures to fix. They're the texture of human maintenance. A collection maintained by a person will have these properties. Acknowledging them is more useful than pretending they can be eliminated.

The five acts of taste and the four human limitations together define what this collection is: a flawed, opinionated, personal map of a field, maintained by someone who cares about it. Its value comes from the caring, not from the perfection.

This collection exists in the gap between human knowledge management — slow, personal, high-judgment — and whatever AI-native knowledge management will look like. It may be a transitional form, something between Bush's Memex and whatever comes after. If so, its value is partly in what it teaches about the transition: what judgment looks like in practice, what gets lost when you automate, what a curated perspective adds to raw retrieval.

The collection doesn't need to be permanent to be useful. It doesn't need to be complete to be valuable. It doesn't need to be perfect to be worth maintaining. It needs to be tended — by someone with taste, with limits, and with the honesty to name both.
