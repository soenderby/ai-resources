# Writing, Research, and the Question of AI Assistance

An outline for a synthesis essay on how to write well, how to research well, and what changes — and what remains unknown — when AI assists with both.

---

## Structure

The essay has three parts. Part 1 covers what we know about writing and research from canonical sources — principles that predate AI and don't depend on it. Part 2 covers what we know about what happens when AI assists with these activities — grounded in empirical evidence, not speculation. Part 3 names the open questions honestly — things we'd need to know but don't yet.

## Part 1: What Good Writing and Research Look Like

This section draws on established sources. Confidence: high.

### Writing is thinking

- The foundational claim, shared by every source in the collection: writing is not the transcription of finished thought but the medium in which thought occurs.
  - Graham: "Writing doesn't just communicate ideas; it generates them."
  - Lamport (widely quoted): "If you're thinking without writing, you only think you're thinking."
  - Booth et al.: "Thinking for others is more careful, more sustained, more insightful — in short, more thoughtful — than just about any other kind of thinking."
  - CGSC guide: "Writing is thinking on paper." (Zinsser, quoted throughout)
- The implication: writing that skips the hard cognitive work — through vagueness, formula, or delegation — skips the thinking. Orwell's version: bad language enables bad thinking, and the process is reversible.

### What makes writing useful

- Graham's four-component formula: useful writing is **true, important, novel to the reader, and stated as strongly as possible** without becoming false. These multiply — weakness in any one collapses the whole.
- The critical tension between precision and correctness: "Vaporous academic writing is correct but vague; the bold rhetoric of demagogues is precise but false. Useful writing is bold, but true."
- The CGSC guide's four standards — **substance, organization, style, correctness** — as a practical checklist. These are interdependent: strong substance in disorganized, unclear prose fails just as badly as polished emptiness.

### The craft of clarity

- Orwell's six rules as negative constraints: avoid dead metaphors, prefer short words, cut mercilessly, use active voice, avoid jargon, break rules when they'd produce barbarous prose.
- Zinsser's positive principle: good writing is a personal transaction between two humans. Simplicity, clarity, brevity, humanity. "The essence of writing is rewriting."
- Pinker's cognitive explanation for why writing fails: the **curse of knowledge** — the writer can't imagine not knowing what they know, so they fail to explain it.
- The CGSC guide's concrete tools: the MEAL paragraph model, side-by-side rewrites, the "tools not rules" philosophy. The most actionable of the sources because it shows rather than tells.

### The craft of research

- Booth et al.'s three-step "So What?" test: (1) I am studying X, (2) because I want to find out Y, (3) in order to help my reader understand Z. If you can't complete step 3, you don't yet know why your work matters.
- Research as conversation between equals: you anticipate objections, acknowledge counterevidence, explain your warrants. The ethics chapter: concealing objections and caricaturing opposing views damage the shared enterprise of understanding.
- Eco's practical principles: read primary sources, not summaries of summaries. "You are not Proust. Do not write long sentences."

### The craft of reading

- Adler's four levels: elementary, inspectional (systematic skimming), analytical (thorough engagement with a single work), syntopical (reading multiple works on the same subject and constructing understanding that no single source contains).
- Syntopical reading is what curation and cross-linking require. It's also what annotation attempts: reading a source analytically, then placing it syntopically against the rest of the collection.
- The key test: are you reading at the analytical level (understanding what the author means, not just what they said) or merely the inspectional level (getting the gist quickly)?

## Part 2: What We Know About AI Assistance

This section draws on empirical evidence. Confidence: moderate to high on mechanisms, lower on practice.

### The cognitive offloading spectrum

- Jazaieri et al.'s taxonomy (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025): **assistive, substitutive, disruptive** offloading. The critical variable is whether the human retains internal monitoring and control — not whether AI is used at all.
  - Assistive: AI helps you organize, outline, check. You retain the thinking. Analogous to a calculator that speeds arithmetic without replacing mathematical understanding.
  - Substitutive: AI replaces the cognitive work. You review output instead of producing it. Creates an "illusion of competence" — users overestimate their understanding of AI-generated content.
  - Disruptive: AI use becomes habitual and unexamined. Self-monitoring diminishes. The human optimizes for speed rather than comprehension without noticing the shift.
- The spectrum is not about the tool but about the mode of use. The same AI can be used assistively (brainstorming) or substitutively (drafting) in the same session.

### What happens to the output

- Durham University physics essay study (Yeadon et al., 2024): 300 essays, half human, half GPT-4. Blinded evaluation found **no statistically significant quality difference**. Markers identified authorship only slightly better than chance.
- Multiple studies show AI output matches or exceeds human output on structural dimensions (organization, grammar, adherence to conventions) while lagging on analytical depth, novelty, and nuanced judgment.
- The implication: if you're evaluating the *product* alone, AI-assisted and human-written output may be indistinguishable. The quality difference — if it exists — is in what the process of writing built in the human, not in what appeared on the page.

### What happens to the human

- The "Google Effect" (Sparrow et al., 2011): people are less likely to remember information they expect to find externally. Extended to AI: if you know the agent will draft the annotation, you process the source less deeply.
- Budapest experiment (Sziklai, 2025): RCT where students with AI access showed disengagement and low understanding of material. When AI was restricted, students escalated to government officials — demonstrating how quickly dependency forms.
- METR study (already in collection): developers believed AI helped them when it actually made them 19% slower. Self-assessment of AI benefit is unreliable.
- Jazaieri et al.: users who rely on AI-generated answers overestimate their knowledge (JOL studies), show lower metacognitive accuracy over time, and shift toward ease over depth.
- The comprehension debt parallel (Osmani, already in collection): the gap between code that exists and code anyone understands. Same mechanism, applied to prose: annotations can exist without anyone having understood the source.

### What happens to language and thought

- "AI's Social Forcefield" (2025): exposure to AI-generated language "shapes not only how people speak, but also how they think, what they attend to, and how they relate to each other." This is empirical evidence for what the garden essay calls annotation drift — the AI's voice literally restructures human cognition.
- The collection's own potential evidence: the annotations in this repo have a consistent analytical voice. Whether that voice belongs to the curator or the AI is indistinguishable from the outside. This is not a theoretical risk.

### The central tension

- The output may be equivalent, but the process builds different things in the human.
- If you care about the *product* (as a reader), AI assistance is unambiguously helpful — it produces clean, well-structured, comprehensive output.
- If you care about what the *process builds in the writer* (understanding, judgment, the ability to think without the tool), AI assistance is a trade-off whose terms are not yet well understood.
- For a personal knowledge base where the curator's growing understanding is the value — not just the annotations on the page — this tension is the whole question.

## Part 3: What We Don't Know

This section names gaps honestly. Confidence in the gap identification: high. Confidence in any resolution: low.

### No validated methodology for AI-assisted writing

- Patterns are emerging: planning-mode use correlates with higher ownership (Emergent Mind survey). Active steering, human direction + AI execution, iterative refinement rather than wholesale generation — these all look promising.
- But no controlled study has tested "this approach to AI-assisted writing produces better outcomes than that approach." The patterns are observations, not validated protocols.
- The CGSC guide's six-stage process (plan, research, draft, revise, edit, submit) was designed for human writers. Whether the same stages apply when AI participates — and which stages benefit from AI vs. which are harmed by it — is an open question.

### No validated methodology for AI-assisted research

- Tools exist: Consensus synthesizes scientific literature, Elicit automates systematic reviews, Undermind mimics human iterative search. Karpathy's autoresearch demonstrates autonomous investigation loops.
- But no source describes a tested, repeatable protocol for human-AI collaborative research — how to divide the work, which judgments to keep human, when to trust and when to verify, how to avoid the substitutive-offloading trap during the research phase itself.
- The deep research paradigm (arxiv survey, 2025) identifies four stages (planning, question developing, web exploration, report generation) but this describes autonomous agent research, not collaborative human-AI research.

### No evidence on whether human curation adds value over AI curation

- The garden essay's central claim — that human curatorial judgment adds something an AI can't replicate — is plausible but unproven.
- Nobody has tested: given the same raw sources, does a human-curated collection produce better outcomes for a downstream user than an AI-curated one? If so, on what dimensions? Accuracy? Insight? Navigability? Taste?
- The Durham physics essay result suggests that for short-form writing, the output quality is comparable. Whether this extends to the more judgment-heavy task of curation (selecting what to include, how to frame it, what to connect it to) is unknown.

### No longitudinal evidence on cognitive effects

- The cognitive offloading research is clear on short-term mechanisms (substitutive use reduces learning and metacognitive accuracy). But we don't know what happens to a curator's judgment after a year of AI-assisted annotation.
- Does the gardener's taste atrophy from AI-assisted curation? Or does it sharpen, because the AI handles mechanical work and frees attention for higher-level judgment? Both are plausible. Neither is tested.
- The Budapest and Durham studies are short-term. The cognitive offloading paper calls for longitudinal research. The field is too new to have produced it.
