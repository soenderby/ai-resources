# Growing the Garden: How This Repo Could Evolve

An essay on the structural evolution of a curated knowledge collection, informed by knowledge management research across six fields — and honest about the tradeoffs embedded in the choices already made.

---

## 1. This Repo Is a Garden

- Mike Caufield's garden/stream distinction (2015): the web is dominated by **streams** (chronological, conversational, self-assertive) at the expense of **gardens** (topological, integrative, iterative).
- This repo is a garden: entries are arranged by conceptual relationship, not publication date. It accumulates knowledge through linking and annotation. The events.md split is a concrete application of Caufield's principle — separating the durable topological layer from the time-ordered stream.
- Vannevar Bush's 1945 Memex vision: the core verbs were **link, annotate, change, copy, summarize** — gardening verbs. Bush imagined a tool for thinking, not a tool for publishing. This repo's annotations do exactly that — they extract, link, and contextualize rather than merely reference.
- The garden metaphor also captures what the repo is *not*: it's not a link dump, not a newsletter archive, not a social feed. It's a deliberately tended space where placement decisions are curatorial judgments.
- But the metaphor has limits. Real gardens are visited by people who didn't plant them. This repo is primarily visited by its own gardener and by AI agents acting on the gardener's behalf. That changes what "tending" means — more on this in section 3.

## 2. Source-Oriented vs. Concept-Oriented: The Central Tension

- Andy Matuschak's strongest principle: **organize by concept, not by source**. When you organize by concept, knowledge accumulates — new material strengthens, complicates, or contradicts what's already there. When you organize by source, you get a scattered collection of summaries that don't compound.
- This repo is organized by source (articles, books, people, projects). The *annotations* extract concepts, but the *structure* doesn't reflect them. To find "everything about agent security" you must scan every annotation.
- **This is a real structural weakness, not just a tradeoff.** Source-oriented organization supports browsing (who wrote what, what's new). Concept-oriented organization supports retrieval (what do I know about X). The repo chose browsability. The cost is that conceptual retrieval — the thing you actually need when you're trying to *think* with the collection — requires reading everything or relying on memory.
- "Both are needed" is not an answer — it's a deferral. If both layers exist, they must be synchronized. The concept layer can go stale independently of the source layer: a new article gets added but the cluster guide doesn't get updated, a concept note links to three sources when there are now seven. Two layers means two maintenance burdens and a synchronization problem. The Zettelkasten tradition mostly sidesteps this by making concept notes *the* primary layer and treating source notes as transient inputs. This repo can't do that without losing the browsability that makes it useful as a shared reference. That's a genuine bind, not a problem with a clean solution.
- The repo already has seeds of a concept layer: the librarian-notes "Connections" section tracks clusters, the ai-generated/ documents synthesize across sources, the annotations use bold terms as concept markers. The honest question is whether these seeds are growing toward something useful or whether they're creating a maintenance surface that nobody will tend.

### 2.1 The human-AI curation partnership

- This garden is unusual: it's co-tended by a human and AI agents. Luhmann's Zettelkasten was designed for one mind. Digital gardens are personal. What happens when the gardener's tool reads everything instantly, never forgets, and produces well-structured prose on demand?
- The failure modes specific to AI-assisted curation are real and mostly unexamined:
  - **Annotation drift**: the AI's voice gradually displaces the human's. Annotations start sounding like each other. The collection develops a uniform synthetic sheen. Are these still *your* annotations, or are they the AI's summaries that you approved?
  - **False comprehension**: the AI produces a well-structured summary. The human reads it, nods, and moves on. The annotation looks like understanding but no genuine processing happened. The collector's fallacy (section 3.1) wearing a more sophisticated disguise.
  - **Comfort-zone reinforcement**: the AI learns the curator's preferences and stops surfacing uncomfortable material. The anti-hype filter becomes an echo chamber filter. The collection's biases deepen without the curator noticing.
  - **Maintenance as performance**: sessions where entries get added, links get tidied, and notes get updated — but no actual thinking happens. The motions of gardening without the substance. The AI makes this seductive because the output *looks* like productive work.
- These connect directly to sources already in the collection: comprehension debt (Osmani) is about the gap between code that exists and code anyone understands — the same gap can open between annotations that exist and annotations anyone has genuinely processed. Supervision fatigue (Pydantic) describes the exhaustion of reviewing AI output that's mostly correct — annotation review has the same structure.
- The uncomfortable implication: the gardener faces the same challenges as the developer working with coding agents. The repo documents these challenges for others but hasn't reckoned with them for itself.

## 3. The Case Against Curation

The strongest objections to this kind of project. Some deserve refutation; some land.

### 3.1 "This is collecting, not learning"

- The **collector's fallacy** (Christian Tietze, Zettelkasten.de): gathering information feels productive but isn't the same as understanding it. You can have a beautifully annotated collection of 70 articles and still not be able to explain the core ideas to someone over coffee without opening the file.
- Tietze's test: can you produce something *new* from what you've collected? If the collection only reproduces what's in it — if the annotations are summaries rather than synthesis — then it's a filing system, not a thinking tool.
- **Partial refutation**: the ai-generated/ essays are evidence of synthesis happening. The "ensuring code quality with agents" essay draws on nearly every cluster in the repo to produce something that doesn't exist in any single source. The librarian-notes track connections that emerge from juxtaposition. This is not *just* collecting.
- **What lands**: the refutation only holds if synthesis *keeps happening*. If the repo enters a phase of pure accumulation — adding entries without producing new understanding — it has become a filing cabinet with nice labels. The essay should be honest that accumulation is the default state, and synthesis is the exception that requires deliberate effort. The risk is not hypothetical — any session that adds five entries and produces zero connections is a session of pure accumulation.

### 3.2 "Just use search"

- If an AI agent can search the entire web and synthesize on demand, why maintain a curated collection at all? This is the strongest objection.
- The case for real-time retrieval: models with million-token context windows can ingest dozens of articles on a topic and synthesize them in seconds. Why pre-process any of this?
- **Refutation, part 1 — quality of annotation**: a search engine finds pages; it doesn't tell you *why they matter* or *how they connect to each other*. The repo's annotations embed curatorial judgment — this is worth reading *because* of this specific insight, and it connects to *that* piece in *this* way. That judgment takes time to form and is lost if you start fresh every time.
- **Refutation, part 2 — the anti-hype filter as curation**: the web is full of noise. A search returns everything. The value of a curated collection is what it *excludes*. The decision that someone doesn't belong, that a popular article is actually shallow, that a speculative piece is below the bar — these negative judgments are invisible but are most of the curatorial work. A search engine can't replicate exclusion decisions.
- **Refutation, part 3 — context over time**: a search gives you the web as it exists today. A maintained collection gives you a *perspective that has evolved*. The librarian-notes entries from March 2026 record decisions and observations that inform future decisions. This temporal depth — the record of how understanding changed — is destroyed by starting fresh each time.
- **What lands — and it lands harder than the refutations suggest**: the three refutations above all assume the annotations are genuinely better than what an AI could produce on the fly from the raw sources. This hasn't been tested. If you gave an agent the 70 original articles and asked it to produce annotations, cross-links, and a synthesis essay, would the result be noticeably worse than what's in the collection? It might not be — and if it isn't, the case for pre-curation collapses to "it's cheaper to maintain than to regenerate," which is an efficiency argument, not a quality argument.
- The honest position: this project is valuable *now*, in the gap between what retrieval can do today and what it will be able to do eventually. The gap is real but closing. The collection may be a **transitional form** — and that's fine. Gardens that accept their impermanence tend to be better maintained than those that assume they'll last forever.

### 3.3 "Your filter is your bias"

- The anti-hype filter is presented throughout this repo as a strength. Every filter is also a blindspot. This section names the blindspots as accepted costs of the chosen approach — not problems to fix, but tradeoffs to own.
- The collection is overwhelmingly male, US/European, practitioner-oriented, and skeptical of commercial enthusiasm. The librarian-notes already flags some of this (gender imbalance, no non-Western perspectives). But flagging a bias isn't the same as correcting it, and correcting it may not be the right move — a personal knowledge base reflects a person's actual sources and interests. Forcing diversity for its own sake would make the collection dishonest. The cost is that the collection's map of the field has large blank regions.
- What the anti-hype filter systematically excludes:
  - **Practitioners who don't publish**: many of the most interesting people building with AI don't write blog posts. They're in private Slacks, internal company docs, and conversations that never become URLs. The collection has a survivorship bias toward people who write publicly. This is structural — a URL-based collection *cannot* capture non-published knowledge. That's a limitation of the medium, not a correctable oversight.
  - **Commercial signal**: some commercial projects and announcements contain genuine signal about where the field is heading. The anti-hype filter risks dismissing the medium (marketing) along with the message (real technical direction). The OpenAI/Astral acquisition piece (now in events.md) is an example of commercial news that carried real signal — but it was admitted because Willison's analysis separated the signal from the marketing. Many commercial announcements with genuine signal won't have a Willison to interpret them.
  - **Enthusiasm as information**: the repo is better at tracking *problems* with AI (fatigue, comprehension debt, security risks) than at tracking *genuine breakthroughs*. Healthy skepticism can shade into reflexive dismissal. A collection that tracks every cost of cheap generation but none of the genuine capabilities risks being as distorted as a collection that tracks only the hype.
- These are not problems to solve. They are the shape of this collection's perspective. A different curator with different interests would produce a different collection with different blindspots. The value is in having an explicit, acknowledged perspective — not in pretending to cover everything.

### 3.4 "AI will make this obsolete"

- Not in the hype sense, but seriously: as model capabilities grow, as context windows expand, as retrieval-augmented generation improves — does a small curated collection still serve a purpose?
- The question has a time horizon. Today, with current tools, the answer is clearly yes. The collection gives a starting agent or a new reader an opinionated map of a vast space. It saves them from rediscovering what the curator already learned.
- In five years? Harder to say. If agents can maintain their own evolving knowledge of the field — reading new publications, updating their understanding, tracking people and projects — then the human-maintained collection becomes a bottleneck rather than an enabler.
- **What to hold onto**: the thing that's hardest to automate is *taste* — the judgment that this source matters and that one doesn't, that this framing is useful and that one is misleading. Taste is accumulated through experience and can't be specified in a prompt. If the collection is valuable, it's because it encodes taste. If taste can eventually be specified or learned, the collection is a transitional artifact.
- The honest position: this is probably a **transitional form** — somewhere between the Memex Bush imagined and whatever comes next. Its value is real and present but not permanent.

## 4. What Happens as Gardens Grow

Problems that emerge from the knowledge management literature, assessed against this repo's trajectory:

### 4.1 Navigation breaks down
- **Information foraging theory** (Pirolli & Card): users follow "scent" — cues that indicate whether a path leads to relevant information. As a collection grows, the scent per entry weakens because there's more to scan.
- The index.md file currently lists all titles — fine at 70 entries, unwieldy at 200. The annotations carry strong scent (bold terms, specific claims), but only if you read them.
- **Maps of Content** (Nick Milo, Obsidian community): navigational hubs that orient a reader to a topic area with curated links and commentary. These are more than indexes — they add context about how entries relate.

### 4.2 Staleness accumulates silently
- **Half-life of knowledge** (Burton & Kebler, 1960): information doesn't disintegrate, it becomes obsolescent. Different types of knowledge decay at different rates — computer science literature has a notoriously short half-life.
- The events.md split is a structural defense: time-bound entries are separated from durable ones. But articles.md entries can also go stale (dead links, abandoned projects, superseded frameworks).
- **Intelligence classification** (security domain): classifiers specify expected duration at creation time. The repo's date convention for events is a version of this, but articles don't carry dates — the implicit assumption is "durable," which may not always hold.
- The Codified Context paper (Vasilopoulos, already in the collection) documents the cost of stale knowledge: "agents trust documentation absolutely." Stale entries in this repo could mislead an agent or a reader.

### 4.3 The concept layer lags behind the source layer
- As more sources are added, the conceptual connections between them multiply faster than the source count grows. With 70 entries, there might be 200 interesting cross-connections. Most of these exist only in the curator's head or in scattered librarian-notes entries.
- The Zettelkasten tradition's answer: make concepts first-class objects. Each concept gets its own note that links to the relevant sources. Over time, the concept notes become the primary navigation layer, and the source notes become evidence.
- The legal database analogy: cases are time-bound events containing durable principles. The West Key Number System extracts principles (headnotes) and organizes them by topic. The source case is found *through* its conceptual classification, not by browsing a chronological list.
- But adding a concept layer introduces the synchronization problem (section 2): two things to maintain instead of one. The mitigation is to let concept notes be born from need (repeated searching for "everything about X") rather than from a completeness impulse.

### 4.4 The collection's boundaries blur
- Every field of knowledge has adjacent fields. This repo started with AI/LLM/agents, expanded into software engineering quality (Parnas, Beck, CUPID), and has now touched knowledge management (Caufield, Matuschak). Where does it stop?
- The durable/perishable skills framework (CLO Magazine) offers a useful lens: **perishable** knowledge is field-specific and decays fast, **semi-durable** knowledge is frameworks and methodology, **durable** knowledge is foundational principles. The adjacent sources in this repo are almost all in the durable or semi-durable category — Parnas (1972), Brooks (1975), Caufield (2015). They justify their place by being more lasting than the AI-specific material they contextualize.
- But there's a risk: **scope creep by quality**. If every strong foundational paper in adjacent fields is in scope, the collection becomes a general-purpose intellectual library. The question "does this illuminate AI agent work specifically?" should remain the gate, not "is this a good piece of writing?"
- An honest example: the Caufield and Matuschak entries added during this session are about knowledge management, not AI agents. They were added because they inform the repo's own structure. That's legitimate for a personal knowledge base — you're allowed to include things that help you think about your tools. But it's also scope creep, and calling it "adjacent to agent context engineering" is a polite rationalization. The essay should name this rather than pretend the boundary held.

### 4.5 The garden can also die

- **What a failing garden looks like** — not just how to grow well but how gardens die.
- **Neglect death**: links rot, annotations reference outdated tools, the index drifts from reality. The collection stays online but becomes misleading. This is the most common mode for personal knowledge projects.
- **Overproduction death**: entries accumulate faster than they can be tended. The gardener adds but never prunes, never revisits, never synthesizes. The collection becomes a filing cabinet — organized but inert. The collector's fallacy (3.1) at structural scale.
- **Abstraction death**: the meta-layers (concept notes, cluster guides, generated essays) grow faster than the primary sources. The repo becomes a collection of commentary about a collection that nobody reads directly anymore. The map replaces the territory.
- **Boredom death**: the gardener's interests shift, the field stops being novel, the daily practice of reading and annotating loses its pull. This is the most honest failure mode and the hardest to plan against. Gardens depend on the sustained interest of a specific person.
- Warning signs: sessions where nothing new is added for weeks. Annotations that feel mechanical. Generated documents that nobody reads. A feed checker that runs but whose output is never acted on. The gardener skimming rather than reading.

### 4.6 Where this collection is right now

- The essay should examine the actual repo, not just describe risks in the abstract.
- **What's working**: the annotations are genuinely high quality — they extract specific ideas, not just topics. The cross-linking between entries creates real information density. The anti-hype filter produces a collection that feels opinionated rather than exhaustive, which is the right quality for a personal knowledge base. The feed checker and agent-assisted workflow make it practical to maintain.
- **What's showing strain**: the source/concept gap (section 2) is real. Finding "everything about X" already requires scanning the full file or relying on the agent's memory of previous sessions. The librarian-notes "Connections" section is becoming a second, less structured index that duplicates information from annotations. The ai-generated/ directory has documents of widely varying quality and currency — some are thorough syntheses, others are research notes from a single session.
- **Where the failure modes are already present**: annotation drift (section 2.1) is arguably already happening — the annotations have a consistent analytical voice that is as much the AI's as the curator's. Whether this is a problem depends on whether the curator has genuinely engaged with the sources or is approving summaries. From the outside, this is indistinguishable — which is part of what makes it insidious. Overproduction risk is moderate: the repo has 76 entries across articles and events after roughly two weeks of active development. That's fast enough that the ratio of adding to thinking about what's already there may be skewed toward adding.
- **What the gardener brings that's bad**: alongside the five acts of taste listed in section 7, the human gardener also brings inconsistency (some annotations are much more detailed than others), availability bias (recently discovered sources get more attention than older entries that might deserve revisiting), fatigue (quality drops in long sessions — the seventh annotation in a batch is rarely as sharp as the first), and forgetting (the curator may not remember what's already in the collection, leading to redundant additions or missed connections).

## 5. Concrete Evolution Paths

Ordered roughly by when they'd become valuable, not by importance:

### 5.1 Staleness review (soon)
- Extend the feed checker or create a new periodic task that audits existing entries: dead links, abandoned projects (no GitHub activity in 6+ months), annotations that reference tools or model versions that no longer exist.
- Lightweight, automatable, directly valuable. The infrastructure already exists in the feed checker.
- The hardest part isn't detecting staleness — it's deciding what to do about it. Remove the entry? Update the annotation? Move it to events.md? Each requires judgment.

### 5.2 Cluster guides (when navigation starts to strain)
- Short documents — one per major conceptual cluster — that link to the relevant articles, events, people, and projects with brief commentary about how they relate. More like annotated reading lists than essays.
- Current clusters worth documenting: agent security, quality with agents, orchestration patterns, labor market effects, judgment memory, software design principles.
- These would be the repo's version of Maps of Content. They could live in ai-generated/ alongside the existing synthesis documents, or in a new guides/ directory.
- The key design question: are these hand-maintained (high quality, high maintenance) or agent-generated (lower quality, lower maintenance)? Probably hand-curated with agent assistance — the curation judgment is the value. But be honest: if the curator won't actually maintain six guides, it's better to have zero than six stale ones.

### 5.3 Topic tags (when the collection outgrows single-file scanning)
- A small controlled vocabulary (~15 terms), added as a comma-separated line per entry. Enough for an agent to filter by topic when loading entries selectively.
- This is the lightest version of **faceted classification** (Ranganathan, library science): describing each item along multiple independent dimensions so it can be found from any angle.
- **The argument for doing it now**: cheap to add per-entry, expensive to backfill later. Every new entry is a missed opportunity to tag.
- **The argument for waiting**: a vocabulary created from 70 entries may not suit 200 entries. The categories you'd choose now may not be the categories that matter later. Premature taxonomy is worse than no taxonomy because it creates false precision.
- **The honest cost of waiting**: if the "wait until it's needed" approach is wrong, you won't know until the collection is large enough that backfilling is painful. The strategy optimizes for avoiding premature structure at the cost of potentially making the right structure harder to add later. This is a real tradeoff, not a free lunch.

### 5.4 Concept notes (long-term, organic)
- Individual concept notes that accumulate over time: `comprehension-debt.md`, `supervision-fatigue.md`, `lethal-trifecta.md`. Each links to the relevant sources and tracks how understanding of the concept has evolved.
- This is the full Matuschak/Zettelkasten move — making concepts first-class objects. It would be the most powerful navigation and retrieval aid, but also the highest maintenance commitment.
- The existing ai-generated/ documents are already proto-concept-notes (judgment-memory.md, for example). The path is to let these grow organically rather than force a restructuring.
- The design principle: **concept notes should be born from need, not from a taxonomy exercise.** When you find yourself repeatedly searching for "everything about X," that's the signal to create a concept note for X.

## 6. What to Resist

### 6.1 Premature structure
- Matuschak: "Premature scaling can stunt system iteration." Every structural addition (tags, concept notes, guides) adds maintenance cost. The cost is invisible at creation but real over time.
- The repo's current philosophy — "suggest structure organically, only propose new directories when there is actual content that warrants them" — is exactly right. The events.md split was warranted by a clear problem (temporal confusion) and a small, clean migration. The same test should apply to future structural changes.
- The test: **is this solving a problem I've actually encountered, or a problem I imagine I might encounter?** Only the former warrants structural change.

### 6.2 Growth itself
- There is probably an optimal size for this collection, and it's not "as large as possible."
- A smaller, well-tended collection where every entry has been genuinely processed and connected is more valuable than a large one where many entries are placeholders for "I should read this properly someday."
- The question to ask before adding an entry is not just "is this good enough?" but "will I actually think about this, or am I just filing it?" If the answer is filing, the entry may pass the quality bar but fail the purpose test.
- At some point, pruning may become more valuable than planting. The sign: when new entries don't change how you think about what's already there.

### 6.3 Over-formalization
- Epistemic status tags, formal citation typing, maturity indicators, confidence scores — all interesting in theory, all adding notation overhead that competes with the annotation prose that makes this collection distinctive.
- **Steelmanning formalization**: formal metadata is machine-readable. An agent can filter by tag; it can't reliably filter by a phrase buried in an annotation. As the collection grows and AI agents become the primary consumers, formal metadata may become genuinely necessary.
- **The counterargument**: the annotations are already doing informally what formal systems do with metadata: noting confidence ("more speculative than the collection's usual bar"), tracking provenance ("builds on X, contradicts Y"), and signaling durability ("coins the term," "one of the foundational papers"). These prose signals are richer than any tag could be.
- **The cost of the current approach**: the "let prose lead until it fails" resolution has a detection problem. If agents reading this collection are silently missing relevant entries because they can't filter by topic or confidence level, the curator may never learn about it. Prose-based signals work when a human reads every annotation; they degrade when an agent skims for relevance. The current approach optimizes for human readability at the expense of machine retrievability. That's a defensible choice, but it is a choice with a cost — and the cost grows as AI agents become more central to how the collection is used.

### 6.4 Automation beyond assistance
- The AI knowledge base management literature is full of automated curation, automated tagging, automated staleness detection. Some of this is genuinely useful (the feed checker). Most of it replaces the judgment that *is* the collection's value.
- **Steelmanning automation**: the gardener's time is finite. If an agent can reliably detect dead links, flag stale project entries, and draft initial annotations from fetched content, the gardener can focus on the high-judgment work: deciding what matters, writing the connections, shaping the perspective. This is the standard coding-agent argument applied to curation.
- **The argument that manual annotation builds taste** — that the act of making curation decisions is how the gardener develops judgment — is plausible but unproven. It might be that taste comes from reading and thinking, not from the mechanical act of writing the annotation, and that delegating annotation would free time for the deeper work that actually builds understanding. The outline takes the position that manual curation preserves taste, but acknowledges this is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated fact. If it's wrong, the repo is paying a significant time cost for no benefit.

## 7. The Garden and the Gardener

- Caufield's essay ends with a call to "put aside your favorite binaries and try out the garden vs. the stream." The repo has already made that choice — it's a garden.
- But a garden needs a gardener. The maintenance question is not optional. As the collection grows, the gardening work grows with it: pruning stale entries, tending cross-links, growing concept notes, reviewing the map against the territory.
- **What the gardener does that the agent can't** — specifically:
  - Decide that a popular, well-written article is actually shallow and doesn't belong.
  - Notice that two unrelated entries are about the same underlying tension and write the connection.
  - Feel that a cluster is getting overrepresented and consciously seek out counterweights.
  - Recognize that their own perspective has shifted and revisit old annotations with fresh eyes.
  - Stop adding for a while and just think about what's already there.
- **What the gardener does that the agent shouldn't have to compensate for** — honestly:
  - Inconsistency: some annotations are thorough three-sentence syntheses; others are perfunctory. Quality varies by session, by fatigue, by how interesting the source was.
  - Availability bias: recently discovered sources get disproportionate attention. Older entries that might deserve revisiting get ignored because they're not in working memory.
  - Fatigue: quality drops over a long session. The seventh entry annotated in a batch is rarely as sharp as the first.
  - Forgetting: the curator may not remember what's already in the collection, leading to redundant additions or missed connections that a machine would catch instantly.
- These are not failures to fix — they're the texture of human maintenance. A collection maintained by a person will have these properties. Acknowledging them is more useful than pretending they can be eliminated.
- The five acts of taste and the four human limitations together define what this collection *is*: a flawed, opinionated, personal map of a field, maintained by someone who cares about it. Its value comes from the caring, not from the perfection.
- **The transitional artifact**: this repo exists in the gap between human knowledge management (slow, personal, high-judgment) and whatever AI-native knowledge management will look like (fast, networked, uncertain). It may be a transitional form — something between Bush's Memex and whatever comes after. If so, its value is in what it teaches about the transition: what judgment looks like in practice, what gets lost when you automate, what a curated perspective adds to raw retrieval.
- The collection doesn't need to be permanent to be useful. It doesn't need to be complete to be valuable. It doesn't need to be perfect to be worth maintaining. It needs to be tended — by someone with taste, with limits, and with the honesty to name both.
