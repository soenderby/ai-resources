# Plan: Entry Structure for Research Organization Methods

## Guiding Principles

### 1. Not all entries deserve equal depth
The document covers ~30 methods. Some are major systems with rich histories and active communities (Zettelkasten, PARA, Commonplace Books). Others are minor techniques best described briefly (Sentence Method, Charting Method). The plan assigns each entry a **tier**:

- **Tier 1 — Full treatment** (~300–600 words): Major systems with significant history, active communities, and distinct philosophies. These deserve the full structural template.
- **Tier 2 — Solid coverage** (~150–300 words): Important methods that are well-defined but narrower in scope or audience. Hit every structural element, but concisely.
- **Tier 3 — Brief sketch** (~75–150 words): Minor techniques, sub-methods, or tools that don't warrant standalone deep treatment. A paragraph or two covering what it is, when to use it, and what distinguishes it.

### 2. Consistent structure within each tier
Every Tier 1 and Tier 2 entry should address the same questions, in roughly the same order. Tier 3 entries can be looser. The structural template:

**Standard entry template (Tier 1 and 2):**

1. **Opening line**: What it is, in one sentence. No preamble.
2. **Origin and history**: Who created it, when, in what context, and why. For older methods, how it evolved. Keep this proportional — a sentence for a minor method, a paragraph for a major one.
3. **How it works**: The concrete mechanics. What does a practitioner actually *do*? What are the structural elements, steps, or components? This is the section most often missing or vague in the current draft.
4. **Who uses it and when**: What kind of work, what kind of person, what kind of problem. Be specific — "academics" is too vague; "PhD students managing 200+ sources for a literature review" is useful.
5. **What it's good at**: Specific strengths. Not generic praise ("very effective") but what particular problem it solves better than alternatives.
6. **What it's bad at**: Honest limitations. Where does it break down, who does it frustrate, what does it fail to do?
7. **Key distinction**: What makes this method different from the thing it's most easily confused with? (Zettelkasten vs. commonplace book; concept map vs. mind map; PARA vs. GTD). Not every entry needs this, but most benefit from it.
8. **Key source(s)**: The canonical text(s) or primary source(s) for someone who wants to go deeper. One or two, not a bibliography.

### 3. Voice and stance
- Write for a reader who is intelligent but may not know the jargon.
- Be direct about limitations. "This method has passionate advocates and genuine weaknesses" is more useful than either hype or dismissal.
- Practitioner perspective over theoretical overview. What does it actually feel like to use this method on a real project?
- When a method has critics, include the criticism — but evaluate it rather than just citing it.

### Section 3: Knowledge Management Systems

#### Zettelkasten — Tier 1
**Current state**: Strong. The best-developed entry in the document.
**What to improve**:
- **Add Luhmann's specific numbering system (Folgezettel)**: The alternating-number system (1, 1a, 1a1, 1a2, 1b, 2...) is how Luhmann implemented branching without folders. This is what makes his method distinct from "just linking notes" and is frequently misunderstood.
- **Add the digital implementation question**: Does Zettelkasten work the same way in Obsidian as it did on paper? Some practitioners (Scott Scheper and the "Antinet" community) argue it does not — that the physical constraints of paper (sequential storage, manual search) are features, not bugs. This is a genuine debate worth noting.
- **Integrate the Goodreads medical doctor critique more fully**: His objection (information-dense fields produce ideas faster than the note-per-idea model can handle) is the strongest empirical pushback against Zettelkasten universalism. It's currently mentioned in passing — give it a sentence more.

#### Building a Second Brain / PARA + CODE — Tier 1
**Current state**: Strong. PARA, CODE, and Progressive Summarization all clearly explained.
**What to improve**:
- **Add what an actual PARA folder structure looks like**: A brief example (Projects: "Q2 report," "Kitchen renovation"; Areas: "Health," "Finances"; Resources: "Marketing," "Cooking"; Archive: completed projects) makes it concrete.
- **Add the "Intermediate Packets" concept**: This is a key Forte idea that's missing — the notion of breaking work into small, reusable units (outlines, drafts, research summaries) that can be recombined across projects. It's what makes BASB more than just a filing system.
- **Sharpen the Progressive Summarization critique**: Include the specific counterargument from Nick Milo (PS produces parrots, not thinkers) and the rebuttal (PS is for discoverability, not understanding — different tools for different jobs).

#### Evergreen Notes / Digital Gardens — Tier 1
**Current state**: Good, but leans theoretical.
**What to improve**:
- **Add "How it works" concretely**: What does a practitioner actually do day-to-day? Read something → write a concept-oriented note → title it as a claim → link it to related notes → revisit and revise over time. This workflow is implied but never stated.
- **Add the digital garden publishing workflow**: Tools like Quartz, Jekyll-garden, Obsidian Publish. How does one go from private notes to public garden? This is a practical question many readers will have.
- **Add Gwern Branwen as a worked example**: His site is the most impressive public digital garden — completion levels, certainty ratings, changelog. It demonstrates what the philosophy looks like in mature practice.

#### GTD — Tier 2
**Current state**: Adequate. Core workflow clear.
**What to improve**:
- **Add the Weekly Review**: This is the beating heart of GTD and it's missing. The weekly review (clear inboxes, review all projects, update next actions) is what keeps the system alive. Without it, GTD decays within weeks.

#### Linking Your Thinking / Maps of Content — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Key insight clearly stated.
**What to improve**:
- **Add a concrete example of a MOC**: "A note titled 'MOC — Decision Making' that links to 15 notes on cognitive biases, decision frameworks, and personal decision logs. The MOC itself contains a brief commentary on how the linked ideas relate." This makes the concept tangible.
- **Add when MOCs fail**: When do they become overhead? When you have too many MOCs, or when you spend more time maintaining navigation than creating knowledge.

#### Ryan Holiday / Robert Greene Notecard Method — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. The waiting-period insight is well-captured.
**What to improve**:
- **Add Greene's role more explicitly**: Greene is the originator; Holiday is the popularizer. Greene's research for books like *The 48 Laws of Power* involved reading 300+ books per project with thousands of cards. This scale is important context.
- **Add the physical outlining step**: The moment where you spread cards on a table and arrange them into a book structure is the method's climax. Describe what this looks like — the spatial, tactile arrangement of hundreds of cards into chapters and arguments.

#### Eco's Index Card System — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Key principles well-captured.
**What to improve**:
- **Add "How it works" as a workflow**: Eco describes a *sequence*: choose topic → preliminary bibliography → find and read sources → make bibliography cards → make reading cards → make ideas cards → organize cards into table of contents → write. This is a complete research workflow, not just a note-taking method. State the sequence.
- **Add the "working hypothesis" concept more fully**: Eco's idea that your table of contents is a hypothesis you test against your evidence as you research is genuinely distinctive. It means the structure of your output evolves with your understanding — you don't fix the outline first and fill it in.

### Section 6: Reading and Comprehension Methods

#### SQ3R — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Steps clearly explained.
**What to improve**:
- **Add its historical significance**: SQ3R (1941) is one of the oldest structured study methods and has spawned many variants (PQ4R, PQRST, KWL). It's the ancestor of most structured reading methods taught today.
- **Add its limitations for research reading**: SQ3R assumes a single text being studied for comprehension. Research reading often involves skimming dozens of sources to find the few worth reading deeply. SQ3R is slow for triage.

#### Adler's Levels of Reading — Tier 2
**Current state**: Clear but thin. Listed as levels without much commentary.
**What to improve**:
- **Expand syntopical reading**: This is the level most relevant to research organization and it gets the least explanation. Syntopical reading means: (1) identify the relevant passages across many books, (2) construct a neutral terminology, (3) define the questions, (4) analyze the discussion, (5) find the truth. This is a methodology, not just "reading lots of books."
- **Add why this matters for organization**: Syntopical reading requires an organizational system — you cannot hold 20 books' positions in your head simultaneously. This is where Adler's method connects to card systems, charting, and concept maps.

#### Feynman Technique — Tier 3
**Current state**: Adequate for Tier 3. Steps clear.
**What to improve**:
- **Add one sentence on its role in the research workflow**: "The Feynman Technique is a processing tool, not a capture or organization tool. Use it after you've gathered and organized material, to test whether you actually understand it."

#### Mind Mapping — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Rules, strengths/weaknesses clear.
**What to improve**:

- **Add when mind maps are genuinely useful in research**: At the start of a project (mapping the territory), when planning a paper structure, when brainstorming connections between disparate topics. Not for dense note-taking or citation management.

#### Concept Mapping — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Distinction from mind mapping well-drawn.
**What to improve**:
- **Add a mini-example**: "Concept map: 'Plants → perform → photosynthesis → requires → sunlight.' This is a proposition — a testable statement made of two concepts and a linking phrase." One example makes the abstract concrete.
- **Add CmapTools**: The specific software (free, from IHMC) that Novak's team built. Worth naming as the canonical tool.

#### KJ Method / Affinity Diagrams — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Process, who uses it, strengths/weaknesses all present.
**What to improve**:
- **Add the "silent" detail more forcefully**: The rule that sorting is done silently, without discussion, is the method's most distinctive feature — it prevents dominant voices from imposing categories and allows the data's structure to emerge. This is mentioned but should be emphasized as the key insight.
- **Add its connection to grounded theory**: Both the KJ method and grounded theory share the principle that categories should emerge from data, not be imposed on it. This connection is worth making explicit.

### Section 8: Research Methodology Approaches

#### Systematic Literature Review — Tier 2
**Current state**: Clear on structure.
**What to improve**:
- **Add PRISMA**: Name the reporting standard. A one-sentence explanation: "PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) is a checklist and flow diagram that standardizes how systematic reviews are reported."
- **Add scoping reviews**: A related but distinct method — scoping reviews map the breadth of a topic rather than answering a specific question. They're faster, less rigid, and increasingly used as a precursor to systematic reviews.
- **Add the organizational demands**: A systematic review produces hundreds or thousands of screened records. How are these managed? (Typically: Covidence, Rayyan, or a spreadsheet.) This connects the methodology back to the document's theme of organization.

#### Grounded Theory — Tier 2
**Current state**: Good. Core method and constant comparative method both explained.
**What to improve**:
- **Add the Glaser/Strauss split**: After their initial collaboration, Glaser and Strauss diverged significantly. Glaser insisted on pure emergence from data; Strauss (with Corbin) introduced more structured analytical procedures. Charmaz later developed constructivist grounded theory. This matters because "grounded theory" means different things depending on which tradition you follow.
- **Add a concrete coding example**: "A researcher interviewing teachers about classroom technology might code a passage as 'time pressure' → group this with 'resource constraints' and 'administrative burden' into a category called 'structural barriers to adoption.'" One example makes the method tangible.

#### Qualitative Coding — Tier 3
**Current state**: Adequate.
**What to improve**:
- **Add deductive vs. inductive coding**: Some researchers start with a codebook (deductive); others let codes emerge (inductive, as in grounded theory). This is the fundamental choice in qualitative data organization.

### Section 10: Conceptual Ancestors

#### The Memex — Tier 1
**Current state**: Good. Vision, influence, and key quote all present.
**What to improve**:
- **Add the "trails" concept as a workflow**: Bush didn't just envision storage and linking — he described a researcher *building trails* through material, then sharing those trails with colleagues. This is essentially curated reading lists or guided tours through a knowledge base. No modern tool has fully implemented this.
- **Add what was never built**: The Memex was never constructed. Bush's team built a Rapid Selector for microfilm, but the full associative-trail system remained conceptual. This matters because it means the Memex is a design document, not an empirical proof.

#### Hypertext — Tier 2
**Current state**: Brief but important.
**What to improve**:
- **Add Nelson's specific criticisms of the Web**: Nelson has argued for decades that the Web implemented hypertext badly — unidirectional links, no version tracking, no transclusion (the ability to embed a passage from one document in another while maintaining the link to the original). These missing features are directly relevant to research organization: broken links, lost sources, and the inability to quote-while-linking are daily frustrations for researchers.
- **Add the connection to backlinks**: Obsidian's backlinks, Roam's block references, and wiki-style bidirectional links are partial implementations of what Nelson originally envisioned. This closes the loop from Section 10 back to Section 3 and 5.

### Section 11: Comparison and Synthesis

**Current state**: Three useful subsections — trade-offs table, choosing-by-context table, combined approach list. The closing insight ("the act of organizing is itself an act of thinking") is strong.

**What to improve**:
- **Add a "lifecycle" view**: Research has phases — exploration, deep reading, analysis, synthesis, drafting, revision. Different methods are suited to different phases. A table mapping methods to phases would be more useful than the current context table, which maps methods to *goals*.
- **Add the "two cultures" observation**: There are fundamentally two philosophies at work — the *curator* approach (carefully select, process, and connect everything; Zettelkasten, Eco, Evergreen Notes) and the *collector* approach (capture broadly, organize minimally, rely on search; PARA, flat files, GTD). Most people lean one way or the other temperamentally, and arguing that one is "right" misses the point.
- **Tighten the combined approach list**: Currently generic. Make it more opinionated — what does a *specific* workflow look like for, say, a PhD student writing a literature-based thesis?
